  • A. Yes, because the sample size is greater than 30.
  • B. Yes, because $n \cdot p = 50(0.4) = 20 \ge 10$ and $n(1-p) = 50(0.6) = 30 \ge 10$.

  • D. No, because the population distribution is unknown.
Q15. (ID: ap_statistics_MCQ_U5S9Q15)
In a recent survey, it was found that 5% of adults in a certain city own a pet snake. If a researcher takes a random sample of 100 adults from this city, why would it NOT be appropriate to use a normal distribution to model the sampling distribution of the sample proportion of adults who own a pet snake?
  • A. Because the sample size is too small.
  • B. Because the proportion $p=0.05$ is too close to 0.
  • C. Because $n(1-p) = 100(0.95) = 95 \ge 10$, but $np = 100(0.05) = 5 < 10$.
  • D. Because the population distribution is skewed.

Q17. (ID: ap_statistics_MCQ_U5S9Q17)
A company claims that 80% of its customers are satisfied with their service. A consumer advocacy group takes a random sample of 150 customers and finds that 105 are satisfied. Assuming the company's claim is true, what is the probability of observing a sample proportion of satisfied customers as low as or lower than the one observed, given the sampling distribution of $\hat{p}$ has a mean of $0.8$ and a standard deviation of approximately $0.0326$?
  • A. $P(\hat{p} \le 0.7) = P(Z \le -3.07)$, which is approximately $0.0011$.
  • B. $P(\hat{p} \le 0.7) = P(Z \le 0.7)$, which is approximately $0.7580$.
  • C. $P(\hat{p} \ge 0.7) = P(Z \ge -3.07)$, which is approximately $0.9989$.
  • D. $P(\hat{p} \le 0.8) = P(Z \le 0)$, which is approximately $0.5$.
