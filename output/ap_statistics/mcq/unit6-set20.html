Q1. (ID: ap_statistics_MCQ_U6S20Q1)

  • A. The probability that more than 30% of adults prefer the new coffee brand is $0.015$.
  • B. If the true proportion of adults who prefer the new coffee brand is 30%, there is a $0.015$ probability of observing a sample proportion as extreme as, or more extreme than, the one observed.

  • D. The probability that the marketing company's sample is wrong is $0.015$.
Q2. (ID: ap_statistics_MCQ_U6S20Q2)
A quality control manager claims that less than 5% of a company's products are defective. A random sample of 200 products finds 6 defective items. A significance test for $H_0: p = 0.05$ versus $H_a: p < 0.05$ yields a p-value of $0.18$. What conclusion should the manager draw at the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level?
  • A. Reject $H_0$ because the p-value is greater than $\alpha$, providing convincing evidence that less than 5% of products are defective.
  • B. Fail to reject $H_0$ because the p-value is greater than $\alpha$, failing to provide convincing evidence that less than 5% of products are defective.
  • C. Reject $H_0$ because the sample proportion $\hat{p} = 0.03$ is less than $0.05$, providing convincing evidence that less than 5% of products are defective.
  • D. Fail to reject $H_0$ because the p-value is less than $\alpha$, failing to provide convincing evidence that less than 5% of products are defective.
Q3. (ID: ap_statistics_MCQ_U6S20Q3)

A test of $H_0: p_A = p_B$ versus $H_a: p_A \ne p_B$ resulted in a p-value of $0.038$. Which of the following is the correct interpretation of this p-value?
  • A. There is a $3.8\%$ chance that fertilizers A and B have different effects on fruit production.
  • B. Assuming there is no difference in the true proportion of plants producing fruit for fertilizers A and B, there is a $0.038$ probability of observing a difference in sample proportions as extreme as, or more extreme than, the one observed.
  • C. If fertilizer A is truly better than fertilizer B, the probability of observing these results is $0.038$.
  • D. There is a $0.038$ probability that the null hypothesis is true.

  • A. Reject $H_0$. There is convincing evidence that Method 1 results in a higher proportion of students passing the exam than Method 2.
  • B. Fail to reject $H_0$. There is not convincing evidence that Method 1 results in a higher proportion of students passing the exam than Method 2.
  • C. Reject $H_0$. There is convincing evidence that Method 2 results in a higher proportion of students passing the exam than Method 1.
  • D. Fail to reject $H_0$. There is convincing evidence that the proportions of students passing are different for the two methods.
