AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 6: Inference for Categorical Data: Proportions (Set 18)

FRQ 1

A large company wants to estimate the proportion of its employees who are satisfied with their current work-life balance. A random sample of 250 employees was surveyed, and 185 reported being satisfied.
a. Identify the population and parameter of interest for this study.
b. Verify the conditions for constructing a one-sample z-interval for a population proportion.
c. Calculate a 95% confidence interval for the proportion of all employees who are satisfied with their work-life balance.
d. Interpret the 95% confidence interval in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies population as all employees and parameter as the true proportion of all employees satisfied.
  • Part b (1pt): Verifies random sample, 10% condition, and Large Counts condition (np≥10, n(1-p)≥10) with calculations using sample proportion.
  • Part c (2pts): Shows correct formula/procedure (z-interval for p) and calculates correct interval (0.686 to 0.794).
  • Part d (1pt): Interprets the interval correctly in context (e.g., "We are 95% confident that the true proportion of all employees satisfied with their work-life balance is between 0.686 and 0.794.").

FRQ 2

A cereal company claims that 30% of its boxes contain a prize. A consumer advocate group suspects the proportion is actually lower. They take a random sample of 150 cereal boxes and find that 38 contain a prize.
a. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses for this situation.
b. Verify the conditions for performing a one-sample z-test for a population proportion.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Based on your p-value, what conclusion should the consumer advocate group draw at a significance level of α = 0.05?
e. Describe a Type I error in the context of this problem and explain a potential consequence.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States H0: p=0.30 and Ha: p<0.30.
  • Part b (1pt): Verifies random sample, 10% condition, and Large Counts condition (np0≥10, n(1-p0)≥10) with calculations using hypothesized proportion.
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates correct test statistic (z-score) and p-value (e.g., z ≈ -0.707, p ≈ 0.2399).
  • Part d (1pt): Compares p-value to alpha and states conclusion in context (e.g., "Since p-value > α, fail to reject H0. There is not sufficient evidence to conclude the proportion is lower than 30%").
  • Part e (1pt): Describes Type I error (reject H0 when H0 is true) in context and a logical consequence.

FRQ 3

Researchers investigated the effectiveness of two different flu vaccines, Vaccine A and Vaccine B. A random sample of 800 people received Vaccine A, and 120 of them contracted the flu. Another random sample of 750 people received Vaccine B, and 150 of them contracted the flu.
a. Identify the appropriate inference procedure for estimating the difference in proportions of people who contract the flu for the two vaccines.
b. Verify the conditions for constructing this confidence interval.
c. Calculate a 90% confidence interval for the difference in the true proportions of people who contract the flu (Vaccine A - Vaccine B).
d. Interpret the confidence interval calculated in part (c) in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies a two-sample z-interval for a difference in proportions.
  • Part b (1pt): Verifies independent random samples, 10% condition for both samples, and Large Counts for both samples (successes/failures for each ≥10).
  • Part c (2pts): Shows correct formula/procedure and calculates correct interval (e.g., -0.057 to 0.007).
  • Part d (1pt): Interprets the interval correctly in context and discusses the meaning of the interval relative to zero (e.g., "We are 90% confident that the true difference in proportions of people contracting flu (Vaccine A - Vaccine B) is between -0.057 and 0.007. Since 0 is in the interval, there may not be a significant difference.").

FRQ 4

A public health organization wants to determine if there is a significant difference in the proportion of adults who smoke in two different regions, Region X and Region Y. A random sample of 600 adults from Region X found 108 smokers. A separate random sample of 550 adults from Region Y found 88 smokers.
a. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses for investigating if there is a difference in smoking proportions between the two regions.
b. Verify the conditions for performing a two-sample z-test for a difference in population proportions.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Based on your findings, what conclusion should the public health organization draw at a significance level of α = 0.01? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States H0: pX = pY and Ha: pX ≠ pY.
  • Part b (1pt): Verifies independent random samples, 10% condition for both samples, and Large Counts for both samples (using pooled proportion for expected counts).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates correct test statistic (z-score ≈ 1.34) and p-value (p ≈ 0.179).
  • Part d (2pts): Compares p-value to alpha, states correct decision (e.g., "Since p-value (0.179) > α (0.01), we fail to reject H0"), and justifies the conclusion in context (e.g., "There is not sufficient evidence to conclude that there is a significant difference in the proportion of adult smokers between Region X and Region Y.").

FRQ 5

A polling organization wants to estimate the proportion of registered voters in a city who support a proposed tax increase. They randomly sample 300 registered voters and find that 165 support the tax increase.
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure for a population proportion and verify the conditions for its use.
b. Construct and interpret a 95% confidence interval for the true proportion of registered voters in the city who support the tax increase.
c. Based on your interval in part (b), does a majority (more than 50%) of registered voters in the city support the tax increase? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly identifies a one-sample z-interval for proportions (1pt); Verifies random sampling, 10% condition (300 < 10% of all voters), and Large Counts (np=165>=10, n(1-p)=135>=10) (1pt)
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates the correct 95% confidence interval (e.g., (0.494, 0.606)) with work shown (1pt); Correctly interprets the interval in context (1pt)
  • Part c (1pt): States a correct conclusion based on the interval (1pt); Justifies using the interval, noting that because 0.50 is contained within the interval, there is not sufficient evidence to conclude that a majority (more than 50%) of registered voters support the tax increase (1pt)
