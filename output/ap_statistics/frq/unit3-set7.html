AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 7)

FRQ 1

A school administrator wants to estimate the proportion of students who support a new school policy. There are 2000 students in the school.
a. Describe how the administrator could use a simple random sample (SRS) of 100 students to gather data.
b. Identify one potential source of bias if the administrator only surveys students who attend an after-school club meeting about the policy.
c. Explain why using an SRS would be preferable to surveying only students at the after-school club meeting.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Explains a valid method for SRS (e.g., assign numbers 1-2000 to students, use a random number generator to select 100 unique numbers, then survey those students).
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies a valid bias (e.g., selection bias, nonresponse bias, undercoverage bias, voluntary response bias) and explains how students at the club might not represent all students.
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that an SRS helps ensure the sample is representative of the entire student body, reducing bias, unlike surveying only club attendees who are likely not representative.

FRQ 2

A researcher wants to study the effect of a new fertilizer on the growth of tomato plants. They have 60 identical tomato plants available.
a. Identify the experimental units in this study.
b. Identify the treatment(s) in this study.
c. Describe how the researcher could incorporate a control group into this experiment.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly identifies the experimental units as the individual tomato plants.
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly identifies the treatment(s) as the new fertilizer (and potentially different levels of fertilizer or a placebo).
  • Part c (2pts): Describes a control group (e.g., a group of plants that receives no fertilizer or receives a standard/placebo fertilizer) and states its purpose (e.g., for comparison to determine if the new fertilizer has an effect).

FRQ 3

Researchers are investigating the relationship between daily coffee consumption and the incidence of heart disease in adults.
a. Describe how an observational study could be conducted to investigate this relationship.
b. Describe how a controlled experiment could be conducted to investigate this relationship.
c. For the observational study described in part (a), explain whether a cause-and-effect conclusion between coffee consumption and heart disease can be established.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes an observational study (e.g., recruit a sample of adults, classify them by daily coffee consumption, and track their heart disease incidence over time).
  • Part b (2pts): Describes a controlled experiment (e.g., randomly assign participants to different coffee consumption groups, control other variables, and measure heart disease incidence).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that a cause-and-effect conclusion cannot be established from an observational study because confounding variables cannot be controlled for, only an association can be shown.

FRQ 4

A company develops a new pain relief medication and wants to test its effectiveness against an existing medication. They recruit 120 volunteers who suffer from chronic headaches.
a. Describe one advantage of using a completely randomized design for this experiment.
b. Explain the purpose of blinding (single-blind or double-blind) in this experiment.
c. Propose a method to randomly assign the 120 volunteers to the two treatment groups (new medication and existing medication) to ensure an equal number in each group.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes an advantage (e.g., helps create groups that are similar on average for confounding variables, reduces bias, simple to implement).
  • Part b (2pts): Explains that blinding helps prevent bias in the perception of effectiveness by participants and/or evaluators (e.g., placebo effect, researcher bias).
  • Part c (2pts): Proposes a valid randomization method (e.g., assign each volunteer a unique number 1-120, use a random number generator to select 60 numbers for the new medication group; the remaining 60 go to the existing medication group).

FRQ 5

In a randomized controlled experiment, 100 patients with a certain disease were randomly assigned to two groups: Group A received a new drug, and Group B received a placebo. After six months, 70% of patients in Group A showed improvement, while 40% of patients in Group B showed improvement. The difference was found to be statistically significant.
a. Interpret the meaning of "statistically significant" in the context of this experiment.
b. Based on these results, can it be concluded that the new drug causes improvement in patients with this disease? Justify your answer.
c. Identify a potential confounding variable that this experiment effectively controlled for through its design.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Interprets "statistically significant" as the observed difference (70% vs. 40%) is unlikely to have occurred by random chance if the drug had no effect.
  • Part b (2pts): Concludes that causation can be established because it was a well-designed, randomized experiment, which helps control for confounding variables, allowing for cause-and-effect conclusions.
  • Part c (1pt): Identifies a valid confounding variable (e.g., severity of disease, age, lifestyle, other medications) that randomization would tend to balance across groups.
