FRQ 1

A survey of 200 college students found that 120 own a laptop (L), 80 own a tablet (T), and 50 own both a laptop and a tablet.

  • Part c (1pt): States that the events are not mutually exclusive and justifies by noting $P(\text{L} \cap \text{T}) \ne 0$ or that there are students who own both.

FRQ 2

A manufacturer of light bulbs states that 15% of its bulbs are defective. A quality control inspector randomly selects 20 bulbs from a large shipment.
a. Justify why a binomial distribution would be an appropriate model for the number of defective bulbs in the sample.
b. Calculate the probability that exactly 3 of the 20 selected bulbs are defective.
c. Calculate the expected number of defective bulbs and the standard deviation of the number of defective bulbs in a sample of 20. Show your work.
d. Interpret the expected number of defective bulbs in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): States and justifies the four conditions for a binomial setting: fixed number of trials ($n=20$), two outcomes (defective/not defective), independent trials, and constant probability of success ($p=0.15$). One point for identifying conditions, one point for justifying in context.
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $P(X=3)$ using binomial formula or calculator as $\binom{20}{3}(0.15)^3(0.85)^{17} \approx 0.2428$ for 1pt.
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates the expected number $\mu_X = n p = 20(0.15) = 3$ for 1pt. Calculates the standard deviation $\sigma_X = \sqrt{n p (1-p)} = \sqrt{20(0.15)(0.85)} \approx 1.5969$ for 1pt.
  • Part d (1pt): States that on average, in many, many samples of 20 bulbs, we would expect 3 of them to be defective.

FRQ 3

A telemarketer makes calls until a sale is made. The probability of making a sale on any given call is 0.08, and calls are independent.
a. What is the probability that the first sale occurs on the fifth call? Show your work.
b. What is the probability that the telemarketer needs 3 or fewer calls to make the first sale? Show your work.
c. Calculate the expected number of calls needed to make the first sale. Interpret this value in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies this as a geometric distribution and calculates $P(X=5) = (0.92)^4(0.08) \approx 0.0573$ for 1pt.
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates $P(X \le 3) = P(X=1) + P(X=2) + P(X=3)$ or $1 - P(X>3) = 1 - (0.92)^3$. Shows work for 1pt and gets correct probability $\approx 0.2259$ for 1pt.
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $E(X) = \frac{1}{p} = \frac{1}{0.08} = 12.5$ calls for 1pt. Interprets that, on average, the telemarketer is expected to make 12.5 calls to achieve the first sale (or similar wording indicating long-run average) for 1pt.

FRQ 5

A company produces a new type of smartphone battery, claiming that 70% of them will last at least two years. A consumer advocacy group wants to investigate this claim by testing a sample of batteries.
a. Describe a simulation using a table of random digits to estimate the probability that, out of 10 randomly selected batteries, fewer than 5 last at least two years. You do not need to perform the simulation.
b. Suppose the advocacy group conducts a simulation with 50 trials, each representing 10 batteries. The simulation results show that in 8 of the 50 trials, fewer than 5 batteries lasted at least two years. Based on these simulation results, what is the estimated probability that fewer than 5 out of 10 batteries last at least two years?
c. Assuming the company's claim is true, calculate the theoretical probability that fewer than 5 out of 10 randomly selected batteries last at least two years.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Assigns digits to outcomes (e.g., 00-69 for lasting $\ge 2$ years, 70-99 for not lasting $\ge 2$ years) for 1pt. Describes how to carry out a trial (selecting 10 two-digit numbers, counting "successes") and how to record the outcome (number of successes < 5) for 1pt.
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates the estimated probability as $\frac{8}{50} = 0.16$ for 1pt.
  • Part c (2pts): Recognizes this as a binomial setting with $n=10, p=0.70$. Calculates $P(X<5) = P(X=0) + P(X=1) + P(X=2) + P(X=3) + P(X=4)$ for 1pt. Provides the correct probability (e.g., using binomialcdf(10, 0.7, 4) or sum of individual probabilities) $\approx 0.0473$ for 1pt.
