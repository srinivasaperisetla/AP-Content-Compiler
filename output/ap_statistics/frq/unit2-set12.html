AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 2: Exploring Two-Variable Data (Set 12)

FRQ 1

A study investigated the relationship between the age of a car (in years) and its selling price (in thousands of dollars). The least-squares regression line for these data is $\widehat{\text{Price}} = 25.5 - 1.2 \times \text{Age}$.
a. Interpret the slope of the regression line in the context of this problem.
b. Interpret the y-intercept of the regression line in the context of this problem. If it is not appropriate to interpret, explain why not.
c. Predict the selling price of a car that is 5 years old.
d. Would it be appropriate to use this model to predict the selling price of a car that is 20 years old? Explain your reasoning.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correct interpretation of slope as an average decrease of $1.2 thousand for each additional year of age.
  • Part b (1pt): Correct interpretation of y-intercept ($25.5 thousand as the predicted price of a new car) and states it is appropriate because age 0 is within the range of data (or implies it).
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates $\widehat{\text{Price}} = 25.5 - 1.2(5) = 19.5$ (or $19,500).
  • Part d (1pt): Explains that it would likely be inappropriate due to extrapolation, as 20 years is likely outside the range of the observed data.

FRQ 5

A statistics class is discussing the relationship between the number of hours students spend studying for a test and their score on the test. They collected data from a sample of students and calculated a correlation coefficient.
a. Explain what a correlation coefficient of $r = 0.92$ would indicate about the relationship between study hours and test scores.
b. Explain why a strong positive correlation, such as $r = 0.92$, does not necessarily mean that studying more hours *causes* higher test scores.
c. Describe a scenario in which the actual relationship between study hours and test scores might be strong but the calculated linear correlation coefficient would be close to zero.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): A strong, positive linear relationship exists between study hours and test scores; as study hours increase, test scores tend to increase.
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that correlation does not imply causation; there could be lurking variables (e.g., student motivation, prior knowledge) that influence both study hours and test scores.
  • Part c (2pts): Describes a non-linear relationship (e.g., quadratic where more study helps up to a point, then too much study decreases scores due to exhaustion or poor allocation of time, or simply a curved pattern) where a straight line would not fit well, leading to a low linear correlation.
