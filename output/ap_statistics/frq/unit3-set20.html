AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 20)

FRQ 1

A high school principal wants to determine student opinion regarding a proposal to extend the school day by 30 minutes. There are 2000 students in the school.
a. Describe how the principal could use a simple random sample (SRS) of 200 students.
b. Describe how the principal could use a stratified random sample of 200 students, stratified by grade level (9th, 10th, 11th, 12th grade). Explain an advantage of using stratified random sampling in this context.
c. Suppose the principal decides to survey students by placing a suggestion box near the main entrance for one week, asking students to drop in their opinions. Identify a potential source of bias in this sampling method and explain how it might affect the results.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes a correct SRS procedure (e.g., assign numbers to all students, use a random number generator to select 200 unique numbers, and survey the corresponding students).
  • Part b (2pts): Describes a correct stratified sampling procedure (e.g., divide students into four grade-level strata, take an SRS from each grade level proportionate to its size) AND explains an advantage (e.g., ensures representation from all grades, reduces variability in estimates, or allows for comparison between grades).
  • Part c (2pts): Identifies a source of bias (e.g., voluntary response bias, undercoverage, or non-response bias) AND explains how it would lead to over- or underestimation of the opinion for/against the proposal (e.g., only students with strong opinions, likely negative, would respond, skewing the results).

FRQ 2

Researchers wanted to investigate the relationship between daily coffee consumption and stress levels among adults. They recruited 500 adults from a local community and surveyed them about their average daily coffee intake and their self-reported stress levels using a standardized questionnaire.
a. Identify the type of study described.
b. Explain why this study cannot establish a cause-and-effect relationship between coffee consumption and stress levels.
c. Based on the study design, to what population, if any, can the researchers generalize their findings? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the study as an observational study.
  • Part b (2pts): Explains that an observational study does not control for confounding variables (e.g., lifestyle, diet, job type, sleep habits) which could influence both coffee consumption and stress levels, thus preventing the establishment of causation.
  • Part c (1pt): States that generalization can only be made to the population of adults similar to those recruited from the local community AND justifies by explaining that participants were not randomly selected from the entire adult population.

FRQ 3

A company develops a new type of fertilizer designed to increase the yield of tomato plants. They want to compare the new fertilizer to their current best-selling fertilizer. They have 60 identical tomato plants available for the experiment.
a. Identify the experimental units, treatments, and response variable in this experiment.
b. Describe how the researchers could incorporate the principle of randomization in this experiment.
c. Explain the purpose of including a control group in this experiment, using the context of the new fertilizer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies the experimental units as the 60 tomato plants, the treatments as the new fertilizer and the current best-selling fertilizer, and the response variable as tomato plant yield (e.g., weight of tomatoes, number of tomatoes).
  • Part b (1pt): Describes a method for randomly assigning the 60 plants to the two fertilizer groups (e.g., assign numbers 1-60 to plants, use a random number generator to select 30 plants for the new fertilizer, and the remaining 30 receive the current fertilizer).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that the control group (the current fertilizer group, in this case) provides a baseline for comparison, allowing researchers to determine if the new fertilizer has an effect beyond what the standard fertilizer provides or any natural growth.

FRQ 4

A university wants to study the effectiveness of two different teaching methods (Method A and Method B) for a challenging statistics course. They have 100 students enrolled in the course, who will be divided into two classes. The university is concerned that students' prior mathematics achievement might influence the effectiveness of the teaching methods.
a. Describe how to design a completely randomized experiment to compare the two teaching methods.
b. Describe how to design a randomized block experiment to compare the two teaching methods, taking into account students' prior mathematics achievement.
c. Explain which of the two designs described in parts (a) and (b) would be more appropriate for this study, and justify your choice.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes completely random assignment of the 100 students to two groups (50 for Method A, 50 for Method B).
  • Part b (2pts): Describes blocking students based on prior mathematics achievement (e.g., high, medium, low) and then randomly assigning an equal number of students within each block to Method A or Method B.
  • Part c (2pts): Chooses the randomized block design AND justifies by explaining that blocking by prior achievement will reduce the variability within treatment groups due to this known factor, allowing for a more precise comparison of the teaching methods by accounting for a potential confounding variable.

FRQ 5

A pharmaceutical company conducts a study to compare a new blood pressure medication (Drug X) with a placebo. They recruit 100 patients with high blood pressure. They randomly assign 50 patients to receive Drug X and 50 to receive a placebo. Nurses administering the medication are aware of which treatment each patient receives, but the patients are not. After three months, blood pressure measurements are taken.
a. Identify the type of blinding used in this study, if any.
b. Identify a potential source of bias in this experimental design and explain how it might affect the observed outcomes.
c. Suppose the study concludes that Drug X significantly lowers blood pressure compared to the placebo. Given the potential bias identified in part (b), interpret this finding with caution.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies that the study uses single-blinding (patients are blind, but nurses are not).
  • Part b (2pts): Identifies that the nurses' knowledge of the treatment (lack of blinding for administrators) is a source of bias AND explains how it could influence results (e.g., nurses might unconsciously give more attention, encourage, or measure blood pressure differently for patients receiving Drug X, potentially inflating the observed effect).
  • Part c (1pt): States that while a significant difference was found, the conclusion should be viewed with skepticism or caution AND explains that the identified bias might have exaggerated the perceived effect of Drug X, making it seem more effective than it truly is.
