AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 6: Inference for Categorical Data: Proportions (Set 6)

FRQ 1

A nationwide poll conducted by a research firm aimed to estimate the proportion of adult Americans who believe that climate change is a serious threat. In a random sample of $1200$ adult Americans, $756$ indicated that they believe climate change is a serious threat.
a. Identify the appropriate inference procedure for constructing a confidence interval for the true proportion of adult Americans who believe climate change is a serious threat. Then, verify the conditions required for this procedure.
b. Construct a $95\%$ confidence interval for the true proportion of adult Americans who believe climate change is a serious threat. Show your work.
c. Interpret the confidence interval constructed in part (b) in the context of this problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies a one-sample z-interval for proportions (1pt); Verifies random sampling, independence ($10\%$ condition: $1200 < 10\%$ of all adult Americans), and large counts ($n\hat{p} = 756 \ge 10$ and $n(1-\hat{p}) = 444 \ge 10$) (1pt)
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates the sample proportion $\hat{p}$ and correctly substitutes values into the confidence interval formula $\hat{p} \pm z^*\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}$ (1pt); Calculates the confidence interval correctly (1pt)
  • Part c (1pt): Interprets the confidence interval in context (e.g., "We are $95\%$ confident that the true proportion of all adult Americans who believe climate change is a serious threat is between X and Y") (1pt)

FRQ 2

A local school district claims that $80\%$ of its high school seniors plan to attend a two- or four-year college after graduation. A local newspaper believes this claim is too high and conducts a survey. They randomly sample $150$ high school seniors from the district and find that $111$ plan to attend college.
a. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses for a significance test to investigate the newspaper's belief. Define any parameters you use.
b. Identify the appropriate inference method and verify the conditions for performing this test.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Based on your results in part (c), what conclusion should the newspaper draw at the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level? Justify your conclusion in context, including an interpretation of the p-value.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States correct null and alternative hypotheses $H_0: p = 0.80$ and $H_a: p < 0.80$, where $p$ is the true proportion of high school seniors planning to attend college (1pt)
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies a one-sample z-test for proportions (0.5pt); Verifies random sampling, independence ($150 < 10\%$ of all high school seniors in the district), and large counts ($np_0 = 150(0.80) = 120 \ge 10$ and $n(1-p_0) = 150(0.20) = 30 \ge 10$) (0.5pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates the sample proportion $\hat{p}$ and the test statistic $z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}$ (1pt); Calculates the p-value (1pt)
  • Part d (2pts): Compares p-value to $\alpha$ and states correct decision (reject/fail to reject $H_0$) (1pt); Justifies decision and interprets the p-value in context of the newspaper's belief (1pt)

FRQ 3

A new manufacturing process is designed to reduce the proportion of defective items produced. The old process had a defect rate of $5\%$. The company wants to test if the new process has a defect rate less than $5\%$. They will sample $200$ items and decide to reject the null hypothesis if the sample proportion of defectives is less than $0.03$.
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for this test. Describe what a Type I error would be in this context and what a Type II error would be in this context.
b. Explain the consequences of a Type I error for the company in this scenario. Explain the consequences of a Type II error for the company in this scenario.
c. Suppose the true proportion of defective items for the new process is actually $0.02$. Describe how increasing the sample size would affect the probability of a Type II error, assuming the significance level remains constant.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (3pts): States $H_0: p = 0.05$ and $H_a: p < 0.05$ (1pt); Describes Type I error as incorrectly concluding the new process is better (defect rate $< 0.05$) when the true defect rate is still $0.05$ (1pt); Describes Type II error as failing to conclude the new process is better (defect rate $< 0.05$) when the true defect rate is actually less than $0.05$ (1pt)
  • Part b (2pts): Explains a plausible consequence of a Type I error for the company (e.g., unnecessary investment in a non-improved process) (1pt); Explains a plausible consequence of a Type II error for the company (e.g., missed opportunity to implement a truly better process, continuing with higher defect rate) (1pt)
  • Part c (1pt): States that increasing the sample size would decrease the probability of a Type II error (0.5pt); Explains why (e.g., larger samples provide more precise estimates and make it easier to detect a true difference/effect if one exists) (0.5pt)

FRQ 4

A marketing firm wants to compare the effectiveness of two different ad campaigns (Campaign A and Campaign B) for a new smartphone app. They randomly assign $400$ potential users to see Campaign A and $350$ potential users to see Campaign B. After a week, $180$ people from Campaign A's group downloaded the app, and $147$ people from Campaign B's group downloaded the app.
a. Identify the appropriate inference procedure and verify the conditions for constructing a confidence interval for the difference in the true proportions of app downloads between Campaign A and Campaign B.
b. Construct a $90\%$ confidence interval for the difference in the true proportions of app downloads ($p_A - p_B$). Show your work.
c. Based on your confidence interval in part (b), does there appear to be a significant difference in the effectiveness of the two campaigns? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies a two-sample z-interval for a difference in proportions (1pt); Verifies random assignment, independence of groups, independence within groups ($400 < 10\%$ of potential users, $350 < 10\%$ of potential users), and large counts for both groups ($n_A\hat{p}_A, n_A(1-\hat{p}_A), n_B\hat{p}_B, n_B(1-\hat{p}_B)$ are all $\ge 10$) (1pt)
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates the sample proportions $\hat{p}_A$ and $\hat{p}_B$ and correctly substitutes values into the confidence interval formula $(\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B) \pm z^*\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_A(1-\hat{p}_A)}{n_A} + \frac{\hat{p}_B(1-\hat{p}_B)}{n_B}}$ (1pt); Calculates the confidence interval correctly (1pt)
  • Part c (2pts): States whether there is a significant difference based on whether the interval contains $0$ (1pt); Justifies the claim in context, explaining what the interval implies about the difference between $p_A$ and $p_B$ (1pt)

FRQ 5

A medical researcher wants to compare the effectiveness of two different medications for treating a certain skin condition. Patients with the condition were randomly assigned to one of two groups. Group A received Medication A, and Group B received Medication B. After one month, the researchers recorded whether each patient showed significant improvement.
GroupNumber of PatientsNumber with Significant Improvement
Medication A12088
Medication B13085
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure and verify the conditions for its use.
b. Calculate a $90\%$ confidence interval for the difference in the true proportions of patients who show significant improvement (Medication A - Medication B).
c. Interpret the confidence interval in context. Based on this interval, is there convincing evidence of a difference in effectiveness between Medication A and Medication B? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly identifies a two-sample $z$-interval for a difference in proportions. Verifies conditions: Random assignment given. For Group A: $n_A\hat{p}_A = 88 \ge 10$ and $n_A(1-\hat{p}_A) = 32 \ge 10$. For Group B: $n_B\hat{p}_B = 85 \ge 10$ and $n_B(1-\hat{p}_B) = 45 \ge 10$. Assume sample sizes are less than $10\%$ of all possible patients (or large populations).
  • Part b (3pts): Calculates $\hat{p}_A = \frac{88}{120} \approx 0.7333$, $\hat{p}_B = \frac{85}{130} \approx 0.6538$. $SE_{\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B} = \sqrt{\frac{0.7333(0.2667)}{120} + \frac{0.6538(0.3462)}{130}} \approx 0.05806$. For $90\%$ CI, $z^* = 1.645$. Interval: $(\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B) \pm z^* SE = (0.7333 - 0.6538) \pm 1.645(0.05806) = 0.0795 \pm 0.0955 = (-0.016, 0.175)$.
  • Part c (2pts): Interprets the interval: "We are $90\%$ confident that the true difference in the proportions of patients showing significant improvement (Medication A - Medication B) is between $-0.016$ and $0.175$." States there is NOT convincing evidence of a difference in effectiveness because the interval contains $0$, meaning it is plausible there is no difference between the two medications.
