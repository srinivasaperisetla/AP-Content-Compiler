AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 14)

FRQ 1

A large urban high school with 2500 students wants to estimate the proportion of students who support a new school policy requiring uniforms. The principal proposes selecting 50 students from each of the four grade levels (9th, 10th, 11th, 12th) and asking their opinion.
a. Identify the sampling method proposed by the principal.
b. Describe how the principal could implement a simple random sample of 200 students from the entire school population.
c. Identify one potential source of bias if the principal were to instead survey only the first 200 students who arrive at school on a given Monday morning, and explain how this bias might affect the estimated proportion.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly identifies the sampling method as a stratified random sample.
  • Part b (2pts): Describes a correct process for a simple random sample, including assigning a unique number to each student and using a random number generator to select 200 students.
  • Part c (2pts): Identifies a relevant source of bias (e.g., convenience sample, response bias, early risers having different opinions) and explains how it would lead to an overestimation or underestimation of the true proportion of support for the policy.

FRQ 2

A pharmaceutical company is developing a new nasal spray to alleviate allergy symptoms. They plan an experiment to test its effectiveness against a placebo. One hundred volunteers suffering from seasonal allergies are recruited for the study.
a. Identify the experimental units, the treatments, and the response variable in this experiment.
b. Describe the purpose of including a placebo in this experiment.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (3pts): Correctly identifies the experimental units (volunteers), treatments (new nasal spray and placebo), and the response variable (e.g., reduction in allergy symptoms, symptom severity score).
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that a placebo helps to account for the placebo effect or provides a baseline for comparison, ensuring that observed effects are due to the medication itself and not psychological factors.

FRQ 3

Researchers are interested in the relationship between childhood exposure to environmental factors and academic performance. Two different studies are described below.
StudyDescription
Study AA group of 500 children is selected at random from a large city. Their lead exposure levels are measured, and their average standardized test scores are recorded for the past three years. Researchers find a negative correlation between lead exposure and test scores.
Study BIn a different study, 200 children with similar academic histories are randomly assigned to two groups. One group lives in homes where environmental hazards (like lead paint) have been professionally removed, and the other group lives in homes where hazards remain. After five years, their academic performance is compared.
a. Identify the type of study for Study A and for Study B.
b. For Study A, identify the appropriate generalizations that can be made based on its findings.
c. For Study B, explain why a causal conclusion regarding environmental intervention and academic performance could potentially be made, assuming ethical considerations are met.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly identifies Study A as an observational study and Study B as an an experiment.
  • Part b (1pt): States that generalizations can be made to the population from which the sample was drawn (e.g., children in similar cities) but not about causation.
  • Part c (2pts): Explains that because subjects were randomly assigned to treatment groups, potential confounding variables are balanced across groups, which allows for conclusions about cause and effect.

FRQ 4

A research team wants to compare the effectiveness of two new dietary supplements (Supplement X and Supplement Y) on increasing muscle mass in adult males. They have 60 male volunteers. The researchers are concerned that age might influence muscle mass gain, with younger men potentially gaining muscle more easily than older men.
a. Describe how the researchers could incorporate blocking into their experimental design to account for the potential effect of age.
b. Explain why it is important to use blocking by age in this experiment.
c. Compare the advantages of a block design over a completely randomized design for this particular study.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Describes forming blocks based on age (e.g., dividing volunteers into age groups like 20-30, 31-40, 41-50, etc.) and then randomly assigning an equal number of individuals from each block to Supplement X and Supplement Y.
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that blocking by age helps to reduce variability within treatment groups and isolate the effect of the supplements by controlling for the known source of variation (age).
  • Part c (2pts): Compares the two designs, stating that blocking increases the precision of the comparison between supplements by reducing the effect of age confounding, whereas a completely randomized design might not balance age groups across treatments by chance, making it harder to determine if observed differences are due to supplements or age.
