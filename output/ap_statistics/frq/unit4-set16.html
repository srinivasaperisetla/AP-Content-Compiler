FRQ 1

A large company conducts a survey about employee satisfaction and their preferred work arrangement. Employees were asked if they preferred working remotely full-time, hybrid, or in-office, and if they reported high job satisfaction. The results are summarized in the table below.
Work ArrangementHigh Job SatisfactionLow Job SatisfactionTotal
Remote12080200
Hybrid15050200
In-office70130200
Total340260600
a. What is the probability that a randomly selected employee prefers a remote work arrangement OR has high job satisfaction?
b. Given that an employee prefers a hybrid work arrangement, what is the probability that they have high job satisfaction?
c. Are the events "prefers remote work" and "high job satisfaction" independent? Justify your answer with calculations.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly calculates $P(\text{Remote} \cup \text{High Sat})$ using the given table and probability rules.
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly calculates $P(\text{High Sat}
  • \text{Hybrid})$ using the given table and conditional probability formula.
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $P(\text{Remote})$ and $P(\text{Remote}
  • \text{High Sat})$ or $P(\text{High Sat})$ and $P(\text{High Sat}
  • \text{Remote})$, or $P(\text{Remote} \cap \text{High Sat})$ and $P(\text{Remote}) \times P(\text{High Sat})$. Provides a correct justification based on the comparison of these probabilities.

FRQ 2

A game involves rolling a fair six-sided die. If a player rolls a 1 or a 2, they win 3 points. If they roll a 3, 4, or 5, they lose 2 points. If they roll a 6, they win 5 points. Let $X$ be the random variable representing the number of points won or lost in one roll of the die.
a. Construct a probability distribution table for $X$.
b. Calculate the expected value of $X$. Show your work.
c. Calculate the standard deviation of $X$. Show your work.
d. Interpret the expected value of $X$ in the context of this game.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly lists all possible values of $X$ and their corresponding probabilities in a table.
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly calculates $E(X) = \sum x \cdot P(X=x)$.
  • Part c (2pts): Correctly calculates $Var(X) = \sum (x - E(X))^2 \cdot P(X=x)$ and then $\sigma_X = \sqrt{Var(X)}$.
  • Part d (1pt): Provides a correct interpretation of $E(X)$ as the long-run average points per roll.

FRQ 3

A manufacturer claims that 85% of their smart light bulbs last for at least 50,000 hours. To test this claim, a quality control engineer randomly selects 15 bulbs for testing. Assume the manufacturer's claim is true.
a. Verify that the conditions for a binomial distribution are met in this context.
b. What is the probability that exactly 12 of the 15 selected bulbs last for at least 50,000 hours? Show your work.
c. What is the probability that at least 13 of the 15 selected bulbs last for at least 50,000 hours? Show your work.
d. Calculate the mean and standard deviation of the number of bulbs (out of 15) that last for at least 50,000 hours.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies the four conditions (fixed number of trials, two outcomes, independent trials, constant probability of success) and explains how each is met in context.
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly calculates $P(X=12)$ using the binomial probability formula or calculator function, showing parameters ($n=15, p=0.85$).
  • Part c (1pt): Correctly calculates $P(X \ge 13)$ as $1 - P(X \le 12)$ or sum of individual probabilities, showing parameters.
  • Part d (1pt): Correctly calculates the mean ($\mu = np$) and standard deviation ($\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}$).

FRQ 4

A salesperson makes cold calls to potential clients. Historically, the probability that a call results in a sale is 0.08. Assume that the outcome of each call is independent. Let $Y$ be the number of calls the salesperson makes until the first sale is achieved.
a. Verify that the conditions for a geometric distribution are met in this context.
b. What is the probability that the first sale occurs on the fifth call? Show your work.
c. What is the probability that the salesperson needs to make more than 10 calls to get the first sale? Show your work.
d. On average, how many calls would the salesperson expect to make to get the first sale? Interpret this value in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies the four conditions (two outcomes, independent trials, constant probability of success, interested in number of trials until first success) and explains how each is met in context.
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly calculates $P(Y=5)$ using the geometric probability formula $P(Y=k) = (1-p)^{k-1}p$.
  • Part c (1pt): Correctly calculates $P(Y>10)$ using $P(Y>k) = (1-p)^k$.
  • Part d (1pt): Calculates $E(Y) = 1/p$ and interprets it as the long-run average number of calls until the first sale.

FRQ 5

A small business sells two types of custom-made shirts: basic shirts and premium shirts. The number of hours to make a basic shirt, $B$, is a random variable with mean $\mu_B = 1.5$ hours and standard deviation $\sigma_B = 0.3$ hours. The number of hours to make a premium shirt, $P$, is a random variable with mean $\mu_P = 2.5$ hours and standard deviation $\sigma_P = 0.5$ hours. Assume $B$ and $P$ are independent.
a. The business receives an order for 2 basic shirts and 1 premium shirt. What is the expected total number of hours to complete this order? Show your work.
b. What is the standard deviation of the total number of hours to complete this order (2 basic shirts and 1 premium shirt)? Show your work.
c. If the cost to make a shirt is $\$10$ per hour plus a fixed cost of $\$5$ per shirt, describe how the standard deviation of the total cost for one basic shirt would compare to the standard deviation of the hours for one basic shirt. Provide a specific calculation for the standard deviation of the cost for one basic shirt.
d. Suppose the business processes a large number of orders, each for 2 basic shirts and 1 premium shirt. Describe how you could use simulation to estimate the probability that a single order takes more than 7 hours to complete.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly calculates $E(2B + P) = 2E(B) + E(P)$ using properties of expectation.
  • Part b (2pts): Correctly calculates $Var(2B + P) = Var(2B) + Var(P) = (2\sigma_B)^2 + \sigma_P^2$ (due to independence) and then takes the square root for the standard deviation.
  • Part c (1pt): Describes that adding a constant does not change standard deviation, but multiplying by a constant multiplies the standard deviation by that constant. Calculates $\sigma_{Cost} = \$10 \times \sigma_B = \$3$.
  • Part d (2pts): Describes a valid simulation process including defining one trial (e.g., generating 2 values from a distribution for B and 1 for P, then summing), how to conduct a trial, and how to estimate the probability (e.g., repeat many times and count proportion of orders > 7 hours).
