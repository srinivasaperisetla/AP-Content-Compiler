AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 2: Exploring Two-Variable Data (Set 17)

FRQ 1

A researcher is studying the relationship between the number of hours a student spends studying for a statistics exam and their score on the exam. Data was collected from 10 students. A scatterplot of the data is shown.
a. Describe the relationship between hours studied and exam score shown in the scatterplot, considering its form, direction, strength, and any unusual features.
b. The correlation coefficient for this data is $r = 0.85$. Interpret this value in the context of the study.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly describes the form (linear), direction (positive), and strength (strong), and indicates no obvious outliers or a clear linear pattern.
  • Part b (2pts): States that there is a strong, positive linear association between hours studied and exam score, meaning as hours studied increase, exam scores tend to increase.

FRQ 2

A company wants to model the relationship between the advertising budget (in thousands of dollars) for a new product and the resulting monthly sales (in thousands of units). The least-squares regression line for their historical data is given by $\widehat{\text{Sales}} = 12.5 + 3.8 \times \text{Budget}$.
a. Interpret the slope of the least-squares regression line in the context of this problem.
b. Interpret the $y$-intercept of the least-squares regression line in the context of this problem. Does it make sense in this context?
c. Predict the monthly sales if the advertising budget is $\$20,000$.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): For every additional thousand dollars spent on advertising, the predicted monthly sales increase by 3.8 thousand units.
  • Part b (2pts): The predicted monthly sales are 12.5 thousand units when the advertising budget is 0 thousand dollars. It may not make sense because a 0 budget might be outside the range of observed budgets or there might be baseline sales not captured by advertising.
  • Part c (1pt): Substitute Budget = 20 into the equation: $\widehat{\text{Sales}} = 12.5 + 3.8(20) = 12.5 + 76 = 88.5$ thousand units.

FRQ 3

A scientist is studying the relationship between the age of a certain tree (in years) and its height (in meters). A least-squares regression line was fitted to the data, and the residual plot is shown.
a. Based on the residual plot, is a linear model appropriate for describing the relationship between tree age and height? Justify your answer.
b. Describe a characteristic of the residual plot that suggests the linear model may not be the most appropriate model.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): No, a linear model is not appropriate. The residual plot shows a clear curved (U-shaped) pattern, indicating that a linear model does not capture the relationship well.
  • Part b (2pts): The residuals are not randomly scattered around 0. Instead, they show a distinct pattern where they are positive for younger and older trees and negative for middle-aged trees (or vice-versa).

FRQ 5

A scatterplot displays the relationship between the number of hours spent exercising per week and resting heart rate (beats per minute) for a sample of individuals. One data point represents an individual who exercises 15 hours per week and has a resting heart rate of 50 bpm. This individual is circled on the scatterplot.
a. Does the circled point appear to be an outlier with respect to the general pattern of the data? Justify your answer.
b. Does the circled point appear to be an influential point for the least-squares regression line? Justify your answer.
c. If the circled point were removed from the data set, would the correlation coefficient ($r$) increase, decrease, or remain approximately the same? Explain your reasoning.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Yes, the point appears to be an outlier because its resting heart rate of 50 bpm is much lower than what would be predicted for someone exercising 15 hours per week, given the negative linear pattern of the other points.
  • Part b (2pts): Yes, the point appears to be an influential point. It has an extreme $x$-value (15 hours) and lies far from the general pattern, suggesting it would pull the regression line towards itself, significantly affecting the slope and/or $y$-intercept.
  • Part c (2pts): The correlation coefficient ($r$) would likely increase (become less negative, closer to 0). The circled point is below the general negative linear trend and has an extreme $x$-value, making the negative correlation appear stronger (closer to -1) than it would be without that point. Removing it would make the remaining points show a weaker negative linear association, thus $r$ would increase towards 0.
