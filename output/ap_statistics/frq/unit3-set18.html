AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 18)

FRQ 1

A large high school wants to estimate the proportion of students who prefer a later start time for classes. The principal proposes two methods for gathering data. Method 1: Randomly select 10 classes and survey all students in those classes. Method 2: Put an announcement in the daily bulletin asking students to visit a specific website to fill out a survey.
a. For Method 1, identify the sampling method used.
b. Explain why Method 1 is an appropriate sampling method for this scenario.
c. Identify and describe one potential source of bias in Method 2.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies cluster sampling.
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that it's random, representative, and practical for large populations because it surveys an entire group which is selected randomly.
  • Part c (2pts): Identifies voluntary response bias AND explains that students with strong opinions (either for or against) are more likely to respond, leading to an over- or under-estimation of the true proportion.

FRQ 2

A pharmaceutical company wants to test a new drug designed to lower blood pressure. They recruit 200 volunteers with high blood pressure and randomly assign them to two groups. One group receives the new drug, and the other receives a placebo. Neither the patients nor the doctors administering the drug know which treatment each patient is receiving. Blood pressure is measured after two months.
a. Identify the experimental units, treatments, and response variable in this experiment.
b. Explain the purpose of using a placebo in this experiment.
c. Describe one benefit of using a double-blind procedure in this experiment.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies experimental units as the 200 volunteers (1pt), treatments as the new drug and the placebo (1pt), and response variable as the change/level of blood pressure after two months (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that the placebo controls for the placebo effect, where patients might show improvement simply because they believe they are receiving treatment, even if it has no active ingredient.
  • Part c (1pt): Describes that a double-blind procedure prevents bias from both the subjects' expectations and the researchers'/doctors' expectations regarding the drug's effectiveness.

FRQ 3

Researchers conducted a study to investigate the relationship between screen time (hours spent on electronic devices) and academic performance (measured by GPA) among high school students. They collected data from a random sample of 500 high school students, recording their average daily screen time and their current GPA.
a. Identify the type of study conducted by the researchers.
b. Can the researchers conclude that increased screen time causes a decrease in academic performance? Justify your answer.
c. Explain whether the results of this study can be generalized to all high school students in the country.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the study as an observational study.
  • Part b (2pts): States that causation cannot be concluded (1pt) AND justifies by explaining that observational studies cannot establish cause-and-effect relationships because confounding variables are not controlled or accounted for (1pt).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that the results can be generalized (1pt) because a random sample of high school students was used, which allows for generalization to the population from which the sample was drawn (all high school students).

FRQ 4

A local supermarket wants to understand customer satisfaction with their new self-checkout system. They place a suggestion box near the self-checkout machines with cards asking customers to rate their experience from 1 (very dissatisfied) to 5 (very satisfied).
a. Identify two potential sources of bias in this method of collecting data.
b. For each source of bias identified in part (a), describe how it might affect the supermarket's estimate of customer satisfaction.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies voluntary response bias (1pt) AND undercoverage (e.g., customers who don't use self-checkout or are in a hurry) OR convenience sampling (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): For voluntary response bias, explains that customers with strong opinions (very satisfied or very dissatisfied) are more likely to respond, potentially over- or under-estimating true satisfaction (1pt). For undercoverage/convenience sampling, explains that the sample might not be representative of all customers, or only those who use self-checkout, leading to a skewed estimate of overall customer satisfaction (1pt).

FRQ 5

A university housing department wants to estimate the proportion of residents who are interested in a new meal plan option. There are 50 dormitories on campus, each housing approximately 100 students. Plan A: Randomly select 5 dormitories and survey every student in those 5 dormitories. Plan B: Obtain a list of all students living on campus and randomly select 500 students from the list to survey.
a. For each plan, identify the specific type of random sampling method described.
b. Explain one advantage of Plan B over Plan A for estimating the proportion of residents interested in the new meal plan.
c. Explain one potential practical disadvantage of Plan B compared to Plan A.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies Plan A as cluster sampling (1pt) and Plan B as simple random sampling (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that Plan B ensures a greater chance of representation from all dormitories/types of students, reducing potential variability or bias from specific dorm clusters, leading to a more precise estimate.
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that Plan B requires more effort/resources to contact and survey individual students spread across many dormitories, which could be less practical or more time-consuming than surveying entire dorms.
