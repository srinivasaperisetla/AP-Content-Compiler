AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 9)

FRQ 1

A school principal wants to survey 100 students to gather their opinions on a new bell schedule. The school has 1000 students total, with 250 in each of grades 9, 10, 11, and 12.
a. Describe how the principal could use a stratified random sample to select the 100 students.
b. Explain one advantage of using a stratified random sample in this context compared to a simple random sample.
c. Suppose the principal decides to survey the first 100 students who arrive at school one morning. Identify the type of sampling method used and describe a potential source of bias with this method.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies strata (grades) AND describes a random selection method within each stratum to achieve the desired proportion (e.g., SRS of 25 students from each grade).
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that stratification ensures representation from each grade level OR reduces variability within strata OR allows for comparisons across strata.
  • Part c (2pts): Identifies method as convenience sampling AND describes specific bias (e.g., early students might differ in opinion from late students, or are more diligent, etc.).

FRQ 2

Researchers investigated the relationship between the amount of sleep high school students get and their academic performance. They collected data from 500 randomly selected high school students, asking them to report their average nightly sleep hours and their GPA from the previous semester.
a. Identify the type of study described.
b. Based on this study, can the researchers conclude that getting more sleep causes higher academic performance? Justify your answer.
c. To what population can the results of this study be generalized? Explain your reasoning.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the study as an observational study.
  • Part b (2pts): States "no" AND justifies by explaining that observational studies cannot establish cause-and-effect due to potential confounding variables.
  • Part c (1pt): Identifies the population as all high school students (or high school students from where the sample was drawn) AND explains this is possible because of the random selection of students.

FRQ 3

A pharmaceutical company is testing a new drug designed to lower blood pressure. They have 120 volunteers with high blood pressure who will participate in the study.
a. Identify the experimental units, the explanatory variable, and the response variable in this experiment.
b. Describe how the researchers could incorporate blinding into the design of this experiment. Explain the purpose of blinding in this context.
c. Suppose the researchers want to compare the new drug to a placebo. Describe how random assignment would be used to form the two treatment groups (new drug and placebo).

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies experimental units (120 volunteers), explanatory variable (type of drug/treatment), and response variable (change in blood pressure).
  • Part b (2pts): Describes blinding (e.g., participants and/or researchers don't know who gets which treatment) AND explains its purpose (to reduce bias in participant response or researcher assessment).
  • Part c (1pt): Describes a method for randomly assigning volunteers to two groups of equal size (e.g., put names in a hat, random number generator) with one group receiving the drug and the other receiving the placebo.

FRQ 4

A company is developing a new training program for its employees to improve productivity. They have 60 employees available for a study and want to compare the new program (Program A) to their current program (Program B). The company believes that employee experience level might affect the outcome, so they categorize employees into "experienced" and "new" (30 in each category).
a. Describe how to set up a completely randomized design for this experiment.
b. Describe how to set up a randomized block design for this experiment, blocking by experience level.
c. Explain one advantage of using the randomized block design described in part (b) compared to the completely randomized design described in part (a).
d. At the end of the study, if a significant difference in productivity is found between Program A and Program B using the randomized block design, what conclusion can be made about causation? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes randomly assigning all 60 employees to either Program A or Program B.
  • Part b (2pts): Describes forming two blocks based on experience level AND randomly assigning employees within each block to Program A or Program B.
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that blocking by experience level reduces variability due to experience, making it easier to detect a difference caused by the programs OR accounts for a known source of variation.
  • Part d (1pt): States that a causal conclusion can be made AND justifies it because of the random assignment used in the experimental design.

FRQ 5

A local town council wants to estimate the proportion of its adult residents who support building a new community center. They decide to mail surveys to 1,000 randomly selected addresses in the town. Only 150 surveys are returned.
a. Identify two potential sources of bias in this study. For each source, explain how it might affect the estimated proportion of support for the new community center.
b. The town council calculates the proportion of support from the returned surveys to be $\hat{p} = 0.70$. They then publicly announce that "70% of town residents support the new community center." Based on your answer to part (a), explain why this conclusion might be misleading.
c. Describe one method the town council could have used to reduce nonresponse bias in their survey.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies two distinct sources of bias (e.g., nonresponse bias, undercoverage if mailing lists are incomplete) AND for each, explains how it would lead to an over- or under-estimation (e.g., those who return surveys are more likely to support the center, leading to overestimation).
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that the conclusion is misleading because of the identified biases, which mean the sample of respondents is not representative of all residents.
  • Part c (1pt): Describes a method to reduce nonresponse (e.g., follow-up reminders, incentives, shorter survey, face-to-face interviews for non-respondents).
