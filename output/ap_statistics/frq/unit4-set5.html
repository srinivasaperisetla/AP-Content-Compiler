AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 4: Probability, Random Variables, and Probability Distributions (Set 5)

FRQ 1

A company manufactures light bulbs, and it is known that 10% of the bulbs produced are defective. An inspector randomly selects 5 bulbs from the production line.
a. Describe how you could use a table of random digits or a calculator to simulate the number of defective bulbs among the 5 selected bulbs.
b. Based on your simulation method in part (a), perform 10 trials of selecting 5 bulbs and record the number of defective bulbs in each trial. Use your results to estimate the probability that exactly 1 of the 5 bulbs is defective.
c. What is the theoretical probability that exactly 1 of the 5 randomly selected bulbs is defective? Show your work using appropriate notation.
d. Compare the probability estimated from your simulation in part (b) to the theoretical probability calculated in part (c).

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes a valid simulation method (e.g., assigning digits 0-9, with 0=defective and 1-9=not defective, for 5 bulbs) for one trial.
  • Part b (2pts): Performs 10 trials correctly and records the number of defective bulbs for each trial; calculates the estimated probability as (count of 1 defective in 10 trials)/10.
  • Part c (2pts): Identifies this as a binomial distribution scenario ($n=5, p=0.10$); calculates $P(X=1) = \binom{5}{1}(0.10)^1(0.90)^4 = 5 \times 0.1 \times 0.6561 = 0.32805$.
  • Part d (1pt): Compares the simulated estimate to the theoretical probability, noting they may differ due to random chance and that simulation is an approximation.

FRQ 3

A recent study found that 60% of adults in a certain city own a smartphone. A random sample of 15 adults from this city is selected. Let $X$ be the number of adults in the sample who own a smartphone.
a. Explain why $X$ can be modeled by a binomial distribution.
b. Calculate the probability that exactly 10 of the 15 sampled adults own a smartphone. Show your work.
c. Calculate the probability that at least 12 of the 15 sampled adults own a smartphone. Show your work.
d. Calculate the mean and standard deviation of $X$. Interpret the mean in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Explains the four binomial conditions: fixed number of trials ($n=15$), two outcomes (success/failure), independent trials (random sample), constant probability of success ($p=0.60$).
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $P(X=10) = \binom{15}{10}(0.60)^{10}(0.40)^{5} \approx 0.1859$.
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $P(X \ge 12) = P(X=12) + P(X=13) + P(X=14) + P(X=15) = \binom{15}{12}(0.6)^{12}(0.4)^3 + \binom{15}{13}(0.6)^{13}(0.4)^2 + \binom{15}{14}(0.6)^{14}(0.4)^1 + \binom{15}{15}(0.6)^{15}(0.4)^0 \approx 0.0634 + 0.0219 + 0.0047 + 0.0005 \approx 0.0905$.
  • Part d (2pts): Calculates mean $\mu_X = np = 15 \times 0.60 = 9$; calculates standard deviation $\sigma_X = \sqrt{np(1-p)} = \sqrt{15 \times 0.60 \times 0.40} = \sqrt{3.6} \approx 1.897$. Interprets $\mu_X=9$ as the expected number of smartphone owners in a sample of 15 adults in the long run.

FRQ 4

An archer shoots arrows at a target. The probability of hitting the bullseye on any given shot is 0.3. Assume each shot is independent. Let $Y$ be the number of shots until the archer hits the bullseye for the first time.
a. Explain why $Y$ can be modeled by a geometric distribution.
b. Calculate the probability that the archer hits the bullseye for the first time on the 4th shot. Show your work.
c. Calculate the probability that the archer takes more than 5 shots to hit the bullseye for the first time. Show your work.
d. Calculate the expected number of shots needed to hit the bullseye for the first time. Interpret this value in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Explains the four geometric conditions: each trial has two outcomes (hit/miss), constant probability of success ($p=0.3$), independent trials, and we are interested in the number of trials until the first success.
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $P(Y=4) = (1-0.3)^{4-1} \times 0.3 = (0.7)^3 \times 0.3 = 0.343 \times 0.3 = 0.1029$.
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $P(Y > 5) = (1-0.3)^5 = (0.7)^5 \approx 0.16807$. (Alternatively, $P(Y>5)=1 - P(Y \le 5)$ and sum probabilities).
  • Part d (2pts): Calculates $E(Y) = \frac{1}{p} = \frac{1}{0.3} \approx 3.333$ shots. Interprets this as, in the long run, the archer is expected to take about 3.33 shots to hit the bullseye for the first time.
