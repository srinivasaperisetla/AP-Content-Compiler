AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 3)

FRQ 1

A high school principal wants to determine student opinion about a proposed change to the school's lunch schedule. There are 1,000 students enrolled in the school. The principal decides to survey 100 students.
a. Describe how the principal could use a simple random sample to select the 100 students.
b. The principal considers surveying the first 100 students who arrive at school on a specific Monday morning. Identify a potential source of bias for this sampling method and explain how it might affect the results.
c. Another proposal is to select 20 students randomly from each of the five grade levels (9th, 10th, 11th, 12th, and unclassified). Identify this sampling method and explain why it might be preferred over a simple random sample in this context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes a correct procedure for a simple random sample (e.g., assigning numbers and using a random number generator).
  • Part b (2pts): Identifies a relevant bias (e.g., convenience sample, early risers, students with certain schedules) and explains the direction of the bias on student opinion about the lunch schedule.
  • Part c (2pts): Correctly identifies the sampling method as stratified random sampling and explains an advantage (e.g., ensures representation of all grade levels, reduces variability within strata).

FRQ 2

Researchers are investigating the relationship between daily exercise and cholesterol levels in adults. They recruit 500 volunteers aged 30-50 and collect data on their average daily exercise duration and their cholesterol levels over a six-month period. They find that individuals who exercise more tend to have lower cholesterol levels.
a. Identify the type of study described. Explain your reasoning.
b. Based on this study, can the researchers conclude that increased daily exercise causes a reduction in cholesterol levels? Justify your answer.
c. Describe how the researchers could design an experiment to investigate whether increased daily exercise causes a reduction in cholesterol levels. Include at least two key components of experimental design in your description.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the study as an observational study and explains that no treatment was imposed.
  • Part b (1pt): States that a causal conclusion cannot be made and justifies that confounding variables (e.g., diet, genetics) may be present in observational studies.
  • Part c (2pts): Describes an experiment including a treatment and control group, random assignment, and measurement of cholesterol levels.

FRQ 3

A pharmaceutical company is developing a new drug to lower blood pressure. They want to test its effectiveness compared to an existing drug and a placebo. They recruit 300 adult patients with high blood pressure who volunteer for the study.
a. Identify the three treatments in this experiment. Briefly describe how these treatments should be assigned to the patients.
b. Explain the purpose of including a placebo in this experiment.
c. Describe how blinding (single-blinding) could be implemented in this experiment and explain why it is important.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies the three treatments (new drug, existing drug, placebo) and describes random assignment of patients to these treatments (e.g., using a random number generator).
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that the placebo accounts for the placebo effect, allowing researchers to distinguish the drug's effect from patient expectations.
  • Part c (2pts): Describes single-blinding (patients don't know which treatment they receive) and explains its importance in preventing bias in patient responses or perceived outcomes.

FRQ 4

A large university wants to conduct a survey to estimate the average amount of money students spend on textbooks per semester. The university has 40,000 students, divided into four colleges: Arts and Sciences (20,000 students), Engineering (10,000 students), Business (7,000 students), and Education (3,000 students). The university plans to survey 1,000 students.
a. Describe how a stratified random sample could be implemented in this context. Calculate the number of students that should be sampled from each college to ensure proportional representation.
b. Describe how a cluster sample could be implemented in this context, assuming the university has 200 sections of a required freshman seminar course, with each section having 200 students. Explain one advantage and one disadvantage of using a cluster sample compared to a stratified random sample for this survey.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Describes stratified sampling by dividing students into strata (colleges) and then taking simple random samples from each. Calculates the number of students for each college: Arts & Sciences (500), Engineering (250), Business (175), Education (75).
  • Part b (3pts): Describes cluster sampling (e.g., randomly selecting a certain number of freshman seminar sections and surveying all students within those sections). States an advantage (e.g., cost-effective, practical) and a disadvantage (e.g., increased variability if clusters are not diverse, less precise than stratified) compared to stratified sampling.

FRQ 5

A horticulturist is testing two new types of soil, Soil A and Soil B, to see which promotes faster growth in a specific species of flower. They have 100 seedlings of uniform size. They want to set up an experiment to compare the average growth height after 8 weeks.
a. Describe a completely randomized design for this experiment. Explain why random assignment is important in this context.
b. Suppose the horticulturist found that after 8 weeks, flowers grown in Soil A had an average height of 25 cm, while those in Soil B had an average height of 22 cm. A statistical analysis revealed a p-value of $0.008$. Interpret this p-value in the context of this experiment and state what conclusion the horticulturist could draw at the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Describes randomly assigning 50 seedlings to Soil A and 50 to Soil B. Explains that random assignment helps create comparable groups, reducing confounding variables and allowing for causal conclusions.
  • Part b (2pts): Interprets the p-value: There is a 0.008 probability of observing a difference in average heights as extreme as 3 cm (or more) if there were no actual difference between the soils. Concludes that there is statistically significant evidence to suggest that Soil A promotes faster growth than Soil B at $\alpha = 0.05$.
