FRQ 1

A company manufactures medical diagnostic tests for a rare disease. For a randomly selected person, the probability of having the disease is $0.005$. The test is $99\%$ accurate for people who have the disease (meaning it gives a positive result $99\%$ of the time). For people who do not have the disease, the test is $98\%$ accurate (meaning it gives a negative result $98\%$ of the time).
a. What is the probability that a randomly selected person tests positive for the disease?
b. Given that a person tests positive for the disease, what is the probability that the person actually has the disease?
c. Interpret the probability calculated in part (b) in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Calculates $P(\text{Positive}) = P(\text{Positive}
  • \text{Disease})P(\text{Disease}) + P(\text{Positive}
  • \text{No Disease})P(\text{No Disease})$ and shows work for $0.005 \times 0.99 + 0.995 \times 0.02 = 0.00495 + 0.0199 = 0.02485$. (1 pt for setup, 1 pt for correct calculation)
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates $P(\text{Disease}
  • \text{Positive}) = \frac{P(\text{Disease} \cap \text{Positive})}{P(\text{Positive})}$ using values from part (a) or similar setup. $\frac{0.00495}{0.02485} \approx 0.1992$. (1 pt for formula/setup, 1 pt for correct calculation)
  • Part c (1pt): Provides a correct interpretation in context, stating that about $19.92\%$ of people who test positive actually have the disease.

FRQ 2

An online retailer receives customer orders throughout the day. The number of specific items ordered, $X$, has the following probability distribution:
Number of Items (x)Probability P(X=x)
00.15
10.30
20.25
30.20
40.10
a. Verify that this is a valid probability distribution.
b. Calculate the mean (expected value) of $X$. Show your work.
c. Calculate the standard deviation of $X$. Show your work.
d. Interpret the mean of $X$ in the context of this problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States that all probabilities are between 0 and 1, inclusive, and that the sum of the probabilities is $1$ (i.e., $0.15+0.30+0.25+0.20+0.10 = 1$).
  • Part b (2pts): Shows calculation for $E(X) = \sum x \cdot P(X=x) = 0(0.15) + 1(0.30) + 2(0.25) + 3(0.20) + 4(0.10) = 0 + 0.30 + 0.50 + 0.60 + 0.40 = 1.8$. (1 pt for setup/formula, 1 pt for correct value)
  • Part c (2pts): Shows calculation for $Var(X) = \sum (x - E(X))^2 \cdot P(X=x)$ or $\sum x^2 \cdot P(X=x) - (E(X))^2$. $Var(X) = (0-1.8)^2(0.15) + (1-1.8)^2(0.30) + (2-1.8)^2(0.25) + (3-1.8)^2(0.20) + (4-1.8)^2(0.10) = 0.486 + 0.192 + 0.01 + 0.288 + 0.484 = 1.46$. $\sigma_X = \sqrt{1.46} \approx 1.208$. (1 pt for setup/formula, 1 pt for correct value)
  • Part d (1pt): States that, on average, the online retailer expects to receive $1.8$ items per order over many, many orders.

FRQ 3

A manufacturer claims that $90\%$ of its new smart light bulbs last at least 10,000 hours. A consumer advocacy group randomly selects 15 of these light bulbs for testing. Assume the manufacturer's claim is true.
a. Verify that this scenario meets the conditions for a binomial distribution.
b. What is the probability that exactly 13 of the 15 selected light bulbs last at least 10,000 hours? Show your work.
c. What is the probability that fewer than 12 of the 15 selected light bulbs last at least 10,000 hours? Show your work.
d. Calculate the expected number of light bulbs that last at least 10,000 hours in a sample of 15. Interpret this value in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the four binomial conditions: (1) Fixed number of trials ($n=15$), (2) Two outcomes (success/failure - lasts/doesn't last), (3) Independent trials, (4) Probability of success ($p=0.90$) is constant for each trial.
  • Part b (1pt): Uses binomial probability formula $P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$ or calculator notation with correct parameters: $P(X=13) = \binom{15}{13}(0.90)^{13}(0.10)^2 \approx 0.2669$. (1 pt for correct value with work/notation)
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $P(X < 12) = P(X \le 11)$. This is $1 - P(X \ge 12) = 1 - [P(X=12) + P(X=13) + P(X=14) + P(X=15)]$ or by summing individual probabilities $P(X=0)$ to $P(X=11)$. Using a calculator, $P(X \le 11) \approx 0.0126$. (1 pt for correct setup/approach, 1 pt for correct value)
  • Part d (2pts): Calculates expected value using $E(X) = np = 15 \times 0.90 = 13.5$. (1 pt for calculation) Interprets this value: On average, if many samples of 15 light bulbs are taken, about $13.5$ of them would last at least 10,000 hours. (1 pt for correct interpretation in context).

FRQ 4

A quality control inspector tests components for defects. Historically, $8\%$ of the components produced by a certain machine are defective. The inspector selects components one by one at random until a defective component is found. Let $Y$ be the number of components inspected until the first defective one is found.
a. Explain why $Y$ follows a geometric distribution.
b. What is the probability that the first defective component is found on the 5th component inspected? Show your work.
c. What is the probability that the inspector has to inspect more than 3 components to find the first defective one? Show your work.
d. Calculate the expected number of components the inspector must inspect to find the first defective one. Interpret this value in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Explains that a geometric distribution models the number of trials until the first success (defective component), with independent trials and a constant probability of success ($p=0.08$) for each trial.
  • Part b (1pt): Uses geometric probability formula $P(Y=k) = (1-p)^{k-1}p$ or calculator notation: $P(Y=5) = (0.92)^4(0.08) \approx 0.0573$. (1 pt for correct value with work/notation)
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $P(Y > 3) = (1-p)^3 = (0.92)^3 \approx 0.7787$. (1 pt for correct setup/approach, 1 pt for correct value)
  • Part d (2pts): Calculates expected value using $E(Y) = \frac{1}{p} = \frac{1}{0.08} = 12.5$. (1 pt for calculation) Interprets this value: On average, the inspector expects to inspect $12.5$ components to find the first defective one. (1 pt for correct interpretation in context).

FRQ 5

A carnival game involves rolling a fair six-sided die. To win a prize, a player must roll a 1 or a 2 on the first roll AND an even number on the second roll. You want to estimate the probability of winning this game using a simulation.
a. Describe a method using a table of random digits to simulate one play of this game. Clearly define what each digit represents and what outcomes constitute a win.
b. Suppose you perform 20 trials of the simulation described in part (a). The results are shown below: \nTrial 1: (3, 4) Trial 2: (1, 6) Trial 3: (5, 1) Trial 4: (2, 2) Trial 5: (6, 5)\nTrial 6: (1, 3) Trial 7: (4, 4) Trial 8: (2, 6) Trial 9: (3, 1) Trial 10: (5, 2)\nTrial 11: (1, 4) Trial 12: (6, 3) Trial 13: (2, 5) Trial 14: (4, 6) Trial 15: (3, 2)\nTrial 16: (5, 4) Trial 17: (1, 1) Trial 18: (6, 2) Trial 19: (2, 3) Trial 20: (4, 5)\nBased on these 20 simulated trials, what is your estimate of the probability of winning the game?
c. Describe how you could improve the accuracy of your estimate from part (b).

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Assigns digits for the first roll (e.g., use single digits 1-6 for die face, ignore 0, 7-9) and for the second roll using independent digits (same assignment). (1 pt for correct assignment and handling of digits). Defines a win: first roll is 1 or 2 AND second roll is even (2, 4, 6). (1 pt for correctly defining win based on assignment).
  • Part b (2pts): Identifies winning trials: Trial 2 (1,6), Trial 4 (2,2), Trial 8 (2,6), Trial 11 (1,4). This is 4 wins out of 20 trials. (1 pt for identifying correct number of wins, 1 pt for calculating probability estimate of $\frac{4}{20} = 0.20$).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that running a larger number of trials (more than 20) would improve the accuracy of the estimate.
