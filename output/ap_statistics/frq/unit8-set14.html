AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 8: Inference for Categorical Data: Chi-Square (Set 14)

FRQ 1

A candy company claims that its bags of multi-colored candies contain 20% red, 20% green, 20% yellow, 20% orange, and 20% blue candies. A curious student believes the distribution is not uniform. The student randomly selects a large bag of 200 candies and counts the number of each color. The observed counts are: Red: 35, Green: 45, Yellow: 40, Orange: 30, Blue: 50.
a. Identify the appropriate inference method and state the null and alternative hypotheses to test the student's belief.
b. Calculate the expected count for each color of candy.
c. Verify the conditions for performing this significance test.
d. The chi-square test statistic for these data is $\chi^2 = 8.75$. Calculate the p-value for this test and interpret it in the context of the problem.
e. Based on your p-value from part (d) and a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$, what conclusion should the student make? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly identifies a chi-square goodness-of-fit test and states correct null and alternative hypotheses ($H_0: p_R=p_G=p_Y=p_O=p_B=0.20$, $H_a:$ At least one proportion is different).
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates the correct expected count for all five colors ($E_i = 200 \times 0.20 = 40$ for each).
  • Part c (1pt): Verifies the conditions: Random sample, Large Sample Size (all expected counts $\ge 5$), and Independence (individual candies' colors are independent).
  • Part d (1pt): Calculates the correct p-value (using $\chi^2 = 8.75$ with $df=4$, p-value $\approx 0.0676$) and interprets it in context (probability of observing data as extreme or more extreme if $H_0$ is true).
  • Part e (1pt): Makes a correct conclusion (fail to reject $H_0$) and justifies it by comparing p-value to $\alpha$ (0.0676 > 0.05) in context, indicating insufficient evidence to conclude the distribution is not uniform.

FRQ 2

A large technology company wants to determine if employee satisfaction levels differ across its three main departments: Engineering, Sales, and Marketing. A random sample of 100 employees is taken from each department, and each employee is asked whether they are "Satisfied," "Neutral," or "Dissatisfied" with their job. The observed counts are summarized in the table below.
SatisfactionEngineeringSalesMarketingTotal
Satisfied605045155
Neutral253540100
Dissatisfied15151545
Total100100100300
a. Identify the appropriate chi-square test to investigate if satisfaction levels differ across departments and state the null and alternative hypotheses.
b. Calculate the expected number of Engineering employees who are "Neutral." Show your work.
c. Verify the conditions for performing this significance test.
d. The calculated chi-square test statistic for these data is $\chi^2 = 10.23$. The p-value is 0.0366. Interpret this p-value in the context of the problem.
e. Based on the p-value and a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$, what conclusion can be drawn about employee satisfaction levels across the three departments? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies a chi-square test for homogeneity and states correct null and alternative hypotheses ($H_0:$ The distribution of satisfaction levels is the same for all three departments; $H_a:$ The distribution of satisfaction levels is not the same for all three departments).
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly calculates the expected count for Engineering-Neutral: $E = \frac{(\text{Row Total}) \times (\text{Column Total})}{\text{Grand Total}} = \frac{100 \times 100}{300} \approx 33.33$.
  • Part c (1pt): Verifies the conditions: Random samples from each population, Large Sample Size (all expected counts $\ge 5$), and Independence within and between samples.
  • Part d (1pt): Interprets the p-value (0.0366) in context as the probability of observing sample differences in satisfaction levels as extreme or more extreme if satisfaction levels are truly the same across departments.
  • Part e (1pt): Makes a correct conclusion (reject $H_0$) and justifies it by comparing p-value to $\alpha$ (0.0366 < 0.05) in context, implying evidence of different satisfaction levels across departments.

FRQ 3

A researcher is investigating whether there is an association between a person's preferred method of exercise and their age group. A random sample of 500 adults was surveyed, and the results are summarized in the two-way table below.
Preferred Exercise18-35 years36-59 years60 and overTotal
Running704015125
Cycling605020130
Swimming407550165
Other10302060
Total180195125500
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for a test to determine if there is an association between preferred exercise method and age group.
b. Calculate the expected number of adults aged "60 and over" who prefer "Swimming." Show your work.
c. Verify the conditions for conducting this significance test.
d. The test statistic is $\chi^2 = 23.45$. Calculate the degrees of freedom for this test. Using this, determine if the p-value is less than 0.01, between 0.01 and 0.05, or greater than 0.05. Explain your reasoning.
e. Based on your answer to part (d) and a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$, what conclusion should the researcher make? Justify your answer in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States correct null and alternative hypotheses for a chi-square test for independence ($H_0:$ There is no association between preferred exercise method and age group; $H_a:$ There is an association between preferred exercise method and age group).
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly calculates the expected count for "60 and over" and "Swimming": $E = \frac{(\text{Row Total}) \times (\text{Column Total})}{\text{Grand Total}} = \frac{165 \times 125}{500} = 41.25$.
  • Part c (1pt): Verifies the conditions: Random sample, Large Sample Size (all expected counts $\ge 5$), and Independence of observations.
  • Part d (1pt): Calculates $df = (\text{rows}-1)(\text{columns}-1) = (4-1)(3-1) = 3 \times 2 = 6$. Using a chi-square distribution with $df=6$, a test statistic of $\chi^2 = 23.45$ yields a p-value $< 0.01$ because $23.45 > 16.812$ (critical value for $\alpha=0.01$).
  • Part e (1pt): Makes a correct conclusion (reject $H_0$) and justifies it by comparing p-value to $\alpha$ (p-value $< 0.01 < 0.05$) in context, implying evidence of an association between preferred exercise method and age group.

FRQ 4

A teacher is analyzing the distribution of letter grades (A, B, C, D, F) in her Statistics class over several semesters. She wants to see if the grade distribution in her most recent class of 100 students is consistent with the historical distribution of 20% A's, 30% B's, 30% C's, 10% D's, and 10% F's. A chi-square goodness-of-fit test is performed.
a. Describe the general shape and properties of a chi-square distribution with 4 degrees of freedom.
b. Explain how the expected number of students receiving a grade of 'A' would be calculated for this test.
c. One condition for performing a chi-square test is that all expected counts must be at least 5. Explain why this condition is important for the validity of the test.
d. Suppose the p-value for the chi-square test statistic calculated for the teacher's data is 0.128. Interpret this p-value in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes the chi-square distribution as skewed right, only taking positive values, and its shape depends on the degrees of freedom (e.g., becomes less skewed as df increases).
  • Part b (1pt): Explains calculation: $100 \times 0.20 = 20$ students. This involves multiplying the total number of students by the hypothesized proportion for A's.
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that the condition ensures that the chi-square approximation to the sampling distribution of the test statistic is reasonably accurate, preventing inaccurate p-values or conclusions due to small expected counts.
  • Part d (1pt): Interprets the p-value (0.128) in context: Assuming the true grade distribution in the recent class is consistent with historical proportions, there is a 12.8% chance of observing a sample distribution as different or more different than the one in the recent class.

FRQ 5

A candy company claims that its bags of multi-colored candies contain 30% brown, 20% yellow, 20% red, 10% orange, 10% green, and 10% blue candies. A curious customer opens a large bag containing 500 candies and counts the following distribution: 160 brown, 110 yellow, 90 red, 50 orange, 55 green, and 35 blue. The customer wants to determine if the observed distribution differs significantly from the company's claimed distribution.
a. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses for a test to address the customer's question.
b. Identify the appropriate inference method and verify the conditions for conducting this test.
c. Calculate the expected number of candies for each color, assuming the company's claim is true.
d. The chi-square test statistic for this data is $\chi^2 = 12.5$. Calculate the p-value for this test and interpret it in context.
e. Based on the p-value from part (d) and a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$, what conclusion can be made regarding the company's claim? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly states $H_0$: The observed distribution of candy colors is consistent with the company's claimed distribution ($p_{brown}=0.30, p_{yellow}=0.20, p_{red}=0.20, p_{orange}=0.10, p_{green}=0.10, p_{blue}=0.10$) and $H_a$: The observed distribution of candy colors is not consistent with the company's claimed distribution.
  • Part b (2pts): Identifies chi-square goodness-of-fit test. Verifies conditions: Random sample (stated or assumed), Expected counts $\ge 5$ (will be confirmed by part c), Number of categories is appropriate.
  • Part c (1pt): Correctly calculates all expected counts (Brown: $0.30 \times 500 = 150$, Yellow: $0.20 \times 500 = 100$, Red: $0.20 \times 500 = 100$, Orange: $0.10 \times 500 = 50$, Green: $0.10 \times 500 = 50$, Blue: $0.10 \times 500 = 50$).
  • Part d (2pts): Calculates degrees of freedom $df = 6-1=5$. Uses $\chi^2 = 12.5$ and $df=5$ to find p-value (e.g., $0.025 < p < 0.05$ or specific calculator value like $p \approx 0.0288$). Interprets p-value correctly in context (e.g., probability of observing a $\chi^2$ statistic as extreme or more extreme as 12.5, assuming $H_0$ is true).
  • Part e (1pt): Compares p-value to $\alpha = 0.05$ and makes correct decision (e.g., $p < 0.05$, reject $H_0$). Justifies conclusion in context (e.g., there is significant evidence that the observed distribution of candy colors differs from the company's claim).
