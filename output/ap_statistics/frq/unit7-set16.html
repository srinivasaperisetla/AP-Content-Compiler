AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 7: Inference for Quantitative Data: Means (Set 16)

FRQ 1

A cereal manufacturer wants to estimate the mean fill weight of its "Crunchy Oats" boxes. A random sample of $n=25$ boxes is taken, and the fill weights (in ounces) are recorded. The sample mean is $\bar{x} = 16.03$ ounces, and the sample standard deviation is $s = 0.15$ ounces. Assume the distribution of fill weights is approximately normal.
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure and verify the conditions for its use.
b. Calculate a 95% confidence interval for the true mean fill weight of "Crunchy Oats" boxes.
c. Interpret the calculated confidence interval in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies a one-sample t-interval for a population mean. Verifies Random sample, Normal population (given), and Independence ($25 < 10\%$ of all boxes).
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates degrees of freedom ($df=24$), finds critical t-value (e.g., $t^* \approx 2.064$), calculates standard error ($SE = 0.15/\sqrt{25} = 0.03$), and computes the interval ($16.03 \pm 2.064 \times 0.03 = (15.968, 16.092)$).
  • Part c (1pt): Correctly interprets the interval: "We are 95% confident that the true mean fill weight of Crunchy Oats boxes is between 15.968 and 16.092 ounces."

FRQ 2

A company claims that its new energy-efficient light bulbs have a mean lifespan of more than 10,000 hours. To test this claim, a consumer advocacy group randomly samples $n=30$ bulbs. The sample mean lifespan is $\bar{x} = 10,150$ hours, with a sample standard deviation of $s = 400$ hours. Assume that the lifespans of these bulbs are approximately normally distributed.
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for a test to investigate the company's claim.
b. Verify the conditions for performing this significance test.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Interpret the p-value in the context of this study and state your conclusion at the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): $H_0: \mu = 10000$ hours, $H_a: \mu > 10000$ hours, where $\mu$ is the true mean lifespan of the new light bulbs.
  • Part b (2pts): Verifies Random sample, Normal population (given), and Independence ($30 < 10\%$ of all bulbs).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates test statistic $t = \frac{10150 - 10000}{400/\sqrt{30}} \approx 2.054$ with $df=29$. Finds p-value (e.g., using calculator/table, $P(t > 2.054) \approx 0.024$).
  • Part d (2pts): Correctly interprets p-value: Assuming the true mean lifespan is 10,000 hours, there is a 0.024 probability of observing a sample mean lifespan of 10,150 hours or more in a sample of 30 bulbs. Concludes: Since $0.024 < 0.05$, reject $H_0$. There is convincing evidence that the true mean lifespan of the new light bulbs is greater than 10,000 hours.

FRQ 3

A consumer research firm wants to evaluate the effectiveness of a new fuel additive. They recruit $n=15$ identical cars and measure their fuel efficiency (miles per gallon, MPG) on a controlled track both before and after adding the additive. The differences in MPG (After - Before) are calculated for each car. The mean difference is $\bar{d} = 1.8$ MPG, and the standard deviation of the differences is $s_d = 0.75$ MPG. Assume the distribution of differences is approximately normal.
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure and verify the conditions for its use.
b. Calculate a 90% confidence interval for the true mean difference in fuel efficiency.
c. Interpret the calculated confidence interval in context and use it to justify whether the additive is effective.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies a one-sample t-interval for a population mean difference (paired t-interval). Verifies Random sample of cars/paired data, Normal distribution of differences (given), and Independence ($15 < 10\%$ of all identical cars).
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates degrees of freedom ($df=14$), finds critical t-value (e.g., $t^* \approx 1.761$), calculates standard error ($SE = 0.75/\sqrt{15} \approx 0.1936$), and computes the interval ($1.8 \pm 1.761 \times 0.1936 = (1.459, 2.141)$).
  • Part c (2pts): Interprets interval: "We are 90% confident that the true mean increase in fuel efficiency due to the additive is between 1.459 and 2.141 MPG." Justifies claim: Since the entire interval (1.459, 2.141) is above 0, there is convincing evidence that the fuel additive significantly increases fuel efficiency.

FRQ 4

A researcher wants to compare the mean reaction times (in milliseconds) of individuals playing a new video game using two different types of controllers, A and B. A random sample of $n_A = 40$ participants uses controller A, resulting in a mean reaction time of $\bar{x}_A = 220$ ms with a standard deviation of $s_A = 25$ ms. Another random sample of $n_B = 35$ participants uses controller B, resulting in a mean reaction time of $\bar{x}_B = 235$ ms with a standard deviation of $s_B = 20$ ms. Assume the distributions of reaction times for both controllers are approximately normal.
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure and verify the conditions for its use.
b. Calculate a 99% confidence interval for the true difference in mean reaction times ($\mu_A - \mu_B$).
c. Interpret the calculated confidence interval in context and explain whether it suggests a significant difference in mean reaction times between the two controllers.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies a two-sample t-interval for a difference of two population means. Verifies two independent random samples, Normal populations (given), and Independence ($40 < 10\%$ of all A users, $35 < 10\%$ of all B users).
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates standard error $SE = \sqrt{\frac{s_A^2}{n_A} + \frac{s_B^2}{n_B}} = \sqrt{\frac{25^2}{40} + \frac{20^2}{35}} \approx \sqrt{15.625 + 11.4286} \approx \sqrt{27.0536} \approx 5.201$. Calculates $\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_B = 220 - 235 = -15$. Determines degrees of freedom (e.g., using conservative $df = \min(n_A-1, n_B-1) = 34$, or calculator $df \approx 72.8$). Finds critical t-value (e.g., for $df=34$, $t^* \approx 2.730$; for $df=72$, $t^* \approx 2.640$). Computes the interval (e.g., $-15 \pm 2.640 \times 5.201 = (-28.73, -1.27)$).
  • Part c (2pts): Interprets interval: "We are 99% confident that the true difference in mean reaction times (Controller A - Controller B) is between -28.73 and -1.27 milliseconds." Explains significance: Since the entire interval is below 0, there is convincing evidence that the mean reaction time for Controller A is significantly lower than for Controller B.

FRQ 5

A farmer is comparing the effectiveness of two different fertilizers, A and B, on corn yield. He randomly assigns 50 plots to fertilizer A and 45 plots to fertilizer B. The corn yield (in bushels per acre) is measured for each plot. For fertilizer A, the mean yield is $\bar{x}_A = 120$ bushels/acre with a standard deviation of $s_A = 10$ bushels/acre. For fertilizer B, the mean yield is $\bar{x}_B = 115$ bushels/acre with a standard deviation of $s_B = 9$ bushels/acre. The farmer wants to determine if fertilizer A leads to a higher mean corn yield than fertilizer B.
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for an appropriate significance test.
b. Verify the conditions for performing this significance test.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Interpret the p-value in the context of this study and state your conclusion at the $\alpha = 0.01$ significance level.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): $H_0: \mu_A - \mu_B = 0$ or $\mu_A = \mu_B$, $H_a: \mu_A - \mu_B > 0$ or $\mu_A > \mu_B$, where $\mu_A$ and $\mu_B$ are the true mean corn yields for fertilizers A and B, respectively.
  • Part b (2pts): Verifies two independent random samples (or random assignment to treatments), Large sample sizes ($n_A=50 \ge 30, n_B=45 \ge 30$, so Normal approximation for sample means is appropriate), and Independence (50 plots < 10% of all possible plots for A, 45 plots < 10% of all possible plots for B).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates test statistic $t = \frac{(\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_B) - 0}{\sqrt{\frac{s_A^2}{n_A} + \frac{s_B^2}{n_B}}} = \frac{120 - 115}{\sqrt{\frac{10^2}{50} + \frac{9^2}{45}}} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{2 + 1.8}} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{3.8}} \approx \frac{5}{1.949} \approx 2.565$. Determines degrees of freedom (e.g., conservative $df=\min(49,44)=44$, or calculator $df \approx 92.4$). Finds p-value (e.g., for $df=44$, $P(t > 2.565) \approx 0.0068$; for $df=92$, $P(t > 2.565) \approx 0.0059$).
  • Part d (2pts): Interprets p-value: Assuming there is no difference in true mean corn yields between the two fertilizers, there is approximately a 0.0068 probability of observing a sample mean difference of 5 bushels/acre or more by chance. Concludes: Since $0.0068 < 0.01$, reject $H_0$. There is convincing evidence that fertilizer A leads to a higher mean corn yield than fertilizer B.
