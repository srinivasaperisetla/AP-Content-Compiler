AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 4: Probability, Random Variables, and Probability Distributions (Set 13)

FRQ 1

A game involves drawing a card from a standard 52-card deck. If you draw an ace, you win $\$10$. If you draw a face card (Jack, Queen, King), you win $\$5$. Otherwise, you win nothing.
a. What is the probability of winning $\$10$ on a single draw? Show your work.
b. What is the probability of winning nothing on a single draw? Show your work.
c. Describe how you would set up and perform a simulation using a random number generator to estimate the average winnings per game if you play 100 games. You do not need to perform the simulation.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Calculates $P(\text{Ace}) = \frac{4}{52} = \frac{1}{13}$ (1pt)
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $P(\text{Nothing}) = 1 - P(\text{Ace}) - P(\text{Face Card}) = 1 - \frac{4}{52} - \frac{12}{52} = \frac{36}{52} = \frac{9}{13}$ (1pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Assigns random numbers to outcomes (e.g., 01-04 Ace, 05-16 Face, 17-52 Nothing) (1pt); Describes simulating 100 games and averaging winnings (1pt)

FRQ 2

An ice cream shop sells three sizes of sundaes: small, medium, and large. The cost for a small sundae is $\$3.00$, a medium is $\$4.50$, and a large is $\$6.00$. Based on past sales data, the probability of a customer ordering a small sundae is $0.40$, a medium is $0.35$, and a large is $0.25$. Let $X$ be the random variable representing the cost of a sundae purchased by a randomly selected customer.
a. Construct a probability distribution table for $X$.
b. Calculate the expected value of $X$, the cost of a sundae purchased by a randomly selected customer. Show your work.
c. Interpret the expected value calculated in part (b) in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Constructs correct probability distribution table with values of $X$ and $P(X=x)$ (1pt)
  • Part b (2pts): Shows calculation $\sum x P(x)$ (1pt); Correctly calculates $E(X) = 3(0.40) + 4.50(0.35) + 6(0.25) = 4.275$ (1pt)
  • Part c (1pt): Interprets $E(X)$ as the long-run average cost per sundae (1pt)

FRQ 3

A bakery sells cupcakes and muffins. The mean number of cupcakes sold per day is $100$ with a standard deviation of $15$. The mean number of muffins sold per day is $70$ with a standard deviation of $10$. Assume the number of cupcakes and muffins sold each day are independent random variables.
a. The bakery sells cupcakes for $\$2.50$ each and muffins for $\$2.00$ each. Calculate the expected total revenue from cupcakes and muffins sold in a day. Show your work.
b. Calculate the standard deviation of the total revenue from cupcakes and muffins sold in a day. Show your work.
c. If the bakery decided to increase the price of all cupcakes by $\$0.50$, describe the effect this would have on the mean and standard deviation of the revenue from cupcakes only.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies expected revenue for cupcakes $E(R_C) = 2.50 \times 100 = 250$ (1pt); Identifies expected revenue for muffins $E(R_M) = 2.00 \times 70 = 140$ and adds them $E(R_{total}) = 250 + 140 = 390$ (1pt)
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates variance for cupcakes $\sigma^2_C = (2.50)^2 \times (15)^2 = 1406.25$ (1pt); Calculates variance for muffins $\sigma^2_M = (2.00)^2 \times (10)^2 = 400$ and adds them, then takes square root for standard deviation $\sqrt{1406.25 + 400} = \sqrt{1806.25} \approx 42.50$ (1pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Explains that the mean revenue would increase by $0.50 \times 100 = 50$ (1pt); Explains that the standard deviation of revenue would remain unchanged (1pt)

FRQ 4

A quality control manager at a factory producing LED light bulbs knows that $5\%$ of the light bulbs produced are defective. A random sample of $20$ light bulbs is selected from the production line. Let $X$ be the number of defective light bulbs in the sample.
a. Explain why $X$ can be modeled by a binomial distribution.
b. Calculate the probability that exactly 2 light bulbs in the sample are defective. Show your work.
c. Calculate the mean and standard deviation of the number of defective light bulbs in a sample of 20. Show your work.
d. What is the probability that at least one light bulb in the sample is defective?

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): States fixed number of trials ($n=20$ bulbs) and two outcomes (defective/not defective) (1pt); States independent trials and constant probability of success ($p=0.05$) (1pt)
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies parameters $n=20, p=0.05$; Calculates $P(X=2) = \binom{20}{2}(0.05)^2(0.95)^{18} \approx 0.1887$ (1pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates mean $\mu_X = np = 20(0.05) = 1$ (1pt); Calculates standard deviation $\sigma_X = \sqrt{np(1-p)} = \sqrt{20(0.05)(0.95)} = \sqrt{0.95} \approx 0.9747$ (1pt)
  • Part d (1pt): Calculates $P(X \ge 1) = 1 - P(X=0) = 1 - (0.95)^{20} \approx 1 - 0.3585 = 0.6415$ (1pt)

FRQ 5

A game show contestant is given a chance to win a prize by rolling a standard six-sided die. To win, the contestant must roll a 3. The contestant gets to roll the die repeatedly until they roll a 3. Let $Y$ be the number of rolls needed to get the first 3.
a. What is the probability that the contestant rolls a 3 on the third roll? Show your work.
b. Calculate the expected number of rolls needed to get the first 3. Show your work.
c. Interpret the expected value calculated in part (b) in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Recognizes geometric probability $P(Y=3) = (5/6)^2 (1/6)$ (1pt); Calculates $P(Y=3) = \frac{25}{216} \approx 0.1157$ (1pt)
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $E(Y) = \frac{1}{p} = \frac{1}{1/6} = 6$ (1pt)
  • Part c (1pt): Interprets $E(Y)=6$ as the long-run average number of rolls required to get the first 3 if the game were played many times (1pt)
