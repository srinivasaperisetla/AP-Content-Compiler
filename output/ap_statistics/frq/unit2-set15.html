FRQ 2

A study investigated the relationship between the weight (in pounds) of a car and its fuel efficiency (miles per gallon, MPG). The least-squares regression line for predicting MPG from weight is $\widehat{MPG} = 45.1 - 0.008 \times \text{Weight}$.
a. Interpret the slope of the least-squares regression line in the context of this problem.
b. Interpret the $y$-intercept of the least-squares regression line in the context of this problem. If the $y$-intercept does not have a practical interpretation, explain why.
c. Predict the fuel efficiency for a car that weighs 3,000 pounds.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Interprets slope as the predicted decrease in MPG for each additional pound of car weight (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Interprets $y$-intercept as the predicted MPG for a car weighing 0 pounds (1pt); Explains that a car weighing 0 pounds is not practical, so the $y$-intercept has no practical interpretation (1pt).
  • Part c (1pt): Shows calculation for prediction ($45.1 - 0.008 \times 3000$) and provides correct predicted MPG (21.1 MPG) (1pt).

FRQ 3

A data analyst is examining the relationship between the amount of time (in minutes) a customer spends on a website and the amount of money (in dollars) they spend on a purchase. The analyst created a scatterplot of the data, the least-squares regression line, and a residual plot.
a. Based on the residual plot, is a linear model an appropriate model for the relationship between time spent on the website and money spent? Justify your answer.
b. One customer spent 15 minutes on the website and spent \$25. If the least-squares regression line predicts a spending of \$22.50 for a customer who spends 15 minutes on the website, calculate the residual for this customer.
c. Describe one advantage of using a residual plot over just the scatterplot to assess the appropriateness of a linear model.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): States that a linear model is not appropriate (1pt); Justifies by describing a clear pattern (e.g., curved pattern, increasing/decreasing spread) in the residual plot (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates the residual as actual - predicted ($25 - 22.50 = \$2.50$) (1pt).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that residual plots magnify deviations from the linear model, making patterns easier to detect than in a scatterplot (1pt).

FRQ 4

A data scientist is studying the relationship between a city's population density (people per square mile) and the average daily commute time (in minutes) for its residents. A random sample of 50 cities was taken, and the correlation coefficient between population density and average daily commute time was calculated as $r = 0.65$.
a. Interpret the correlation coefficient $r=0.65$ in the context of this study.
b. Explain what it means if a point on the scatterplot of population density versus commute time is considered an "influential point."
c. Describe one condition under which it would NOT be appropriate to calculate a correlation coefficient for a set of bivariate data, even if both variables are quantitative.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Interprets $r$ in context, mentioning moderate, positive linear association between population density and commute time (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Explains that an influential point is an observation that, if removed, would significantly change the slope and/or $y$-intercept of the least-squares regression line (1pt); Mentions that it often has a large $x$-value (leverage) and may or may not have a large residual (1pt).
  • Part c (1pt): Describes a situation where the relationship is clearly nonlinear, or there are extreme outliers that distort the linear relationship (1pt).

FRQ 5

A marine biologist is studying the relationship between the length (in cm) and weight (in grams) of a certain species of fish. A scatterplot of weight versus length showed a curved pattern. To linearize the relationship, the biologist transformed the weight data using the natural logarithm. The least-squares regression line for the transformed data is $\widehat{\ln(\text{Weight})} = -3.5 + 1.8 \times \text{Length}$. The coefficient of determination ($r^2$) for this transformed model is $0.92$.
a. Calculate the predicted weight (in grams) for a fish with a length of 10 cm.
b. Interpret the value of $r^2 = 0.92$ in the context of this problem.
c. Explain why the biologist chose to transform the data rather than directly fitting a linear model to the original length and weight data.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Shows calculation for $\widehat{\ln(\text{Weight})} = -3.5 + 1.8 \times 10 = 14.5$ (1pt); Calculates the predicted weight by exponentiating: $e^{14.5} \approx 728896$ grams (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Interprets $r^2$ as 92% of the variation in the natural logarithm of fish weight can be explained by the linear relationship with fish length (1pt).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that a linear model was not appropriate for the original data because the scatterplot showed a curved (nonlinear) pattern, and transformation helped linearize the relationship (1pt).
