AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 6: Inference for Categorical Data: Proportions (Set 3)

FRQ 1

A high school administration wants to estimate the proportion of students who feel safe in the school hallways. They take a simple random sample of $150$ students and find that $123$ of them report feeling safe.
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure for estimating the true proportion of students who feel safe, and verify the conditions for its use.
b. Calculate a $95\%$ confidence interval for the true proportion of all students who feel safe in the school hallways.
c. Interpret the calculated confidence interval in context. Based on this interval, can the administration claim that more than $75\%$ of students feel safe? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies a one-sample $z$-interval for a proportion (1 pt); Verifies conditions: Random sample (given), $150(123/150) = 123 \ge 10$ and $150(27/150) = 27 \ge 10$ (1 pt)
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates the interval $\hat{p} \pm z^* \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = 0.82 \pm 1.96 \sqrt{\frac{0.82(0.18)}{150}}$ leading to $(0.760, 0.880)$ (1 pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Interprets the interval as being $95\%$ confident the true proportion is between $0.760$ and $0.880$ (1 pt); Justifies that since $0.75$ is below the lower bound of the interval, it is plausible that more than $75\%$ feel safe (1 pt)

FRQ 2

A company claims that at least $8\%$ of its light bulbs fail quality control. A consumer advocacy group suspects the proportion of bulbs failing quality control is lower. They take a random sample of $200$ light bulbs and find $10$ bulbs that fail quality control.
a. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses for a significance test to investigate the consumer group's suspicion. Identify the appropriate inference method.
b. Verify the conditions for performing this significance test.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Based on the p-value calculated in part (c) and using a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$, what conclusion should the consumer advocacy group make about the company's claim? Interpret the p-value in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): States $H_0: p = 0.08$ and $H_a: p < 0.08$ (1 pt); Identifies a one-sample $z$-test for a proportion (1 pt)
  • Part b (1pt): Verifies conditions: Random sample (given), $n p_0 = 200(0.08) = 16 \ge 10$ and $n(1-p_0) = 200(0.92) = 184 \ge 10$ (1 pt)
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates $\hat{p} = 10/200 = 0.05$. Calculates test statistic $z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}} = \frac{0.05 - 0.08}{\sqrt{\frac{0.08(0.92)}{200}}} \approx \frac{-0.03}{0.0192} \approx -1.56$ and p-value $P(Z < -1.56) \approx 0.0594$ (1 pt)
  • Part d (2pts): Interprets the p-value: The probability of observing a sample proportion of $0.05$ or less, assuming the true proportion of defective bulbs is $0.08$, is approximately $0.0594$ (1 pt). Since $p\text{-value} = 0.0594 > \alpha = 0.05$, the consumer group should fail to reject $H_0$. There is not sufficient evidence to conclude that the proportion of bulbs failing quality control is lower than $8\%$ (1 pt).

FRQ 3

A marketing company wants to compare the effectiveness of two different social media ad campaigns (Campaign A and Campaign B) in encouraging online purchases. They randomly assign $300$ potential customers to view Campaign A and $320$ potential customers to view Campaign B. For Campaign A, $60$ customers made a purchase, and for Campaign B, $75$ customers made a purchase.
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure for estimating the difference in the true proportions of purchases for the two campaigns, and verify the conditions for its use.
b. Calculate a $90\%$ confidence interval for the difference in the true proportions of customers making a purchase ($p_A - p_B$).
c. Interpret the calculated confidence interval in context. Based on this interval, does there appear to be a significant difference in the effectiveness of the two campaigns? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies a two-sample $z$-interval for a difference in proportions (1 pt); Verifies conditions: Two independent random samples (given), and for Campaign A: $n_A\hat{p}_A = 60 \ge 10$, $n_A(1-\hat{p}_A) = 240 \ge 10$; for Campaign B: $n_B\hat{p}_B = 75 \ge 10$, $n_B(1-\hat{p}_B) = 245 \ge 10$ (1 pt)
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $\hat{p}_A = 60/300 = 0.20$ and $\hat{p}_B = 75/320 \approx 0.234375$. Calculates interval $(\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B) \pm z^* \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_A(1-\hat{p}_A)}{n_A} + \frac{\hat{p}_B(1-\hat{p}_B)}{n_B}} = (0.20 - 0.234375) \pm 1.645 \sqrt{\frac{0.20(0.80)}{300} + \frac{0.234375(0.765625)}{320}} \approx -0.034375 \pm 0.05436$ leading to $(-0.0887, 0.0200)$ (1 pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Interprets the interval as being $90\%$ confident the true difference in proportions ($p_A - p_B$) is between $-0.0887$ and $0.0200$ (1 pt); Justifies that since $0$ is included in the interval, there is no statistically significant difference between the two campaigns at the $0.10$ level (1 pt).

FRQ 4

A health organization wants to determine if there is a difference in the proportion of adults who regularly exercise between urban and rural areas. They conduct a nationwide survey, taking a simple random sample of $400$ adults from urban areas and $350$ adults from rural areas. In the urban sample, $180$ adults reported regular exercise, while in the rural sample, $140$ adults reported regular exercise.
a. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses to test if there is a difference in the true proportions of adults who regularly exercise between urban and rural areas. Identify the appropriate inference method.
b. Verify the conditions for performing this significance test.
c. Calculate the test statistic for this test.
d. The p-value for this test is approximately $0.113$. Based on this p-value and a significance level of $\alpha = 0.10$, what conclusion should the health organization make? Interpret the p-value in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): States $H_0: p_U - p_R = 0$ (or $p_U = p_R$) and $H_a: p_U - p_R \ne 0$ (or $p_U \ne p_R$) (1 pt); Identifies a two-sample $z$-test for a difference in proportions (1 pt)
  • Part b (1pt): Verifies conditions: Two independent random samples (given), and for Urban: $n_U\hat{p}_U = 180 \ge 10$, $n_U(1-\hat{p}_U) = 220 \ge 10$; for Rural: $n_R\hat{p}_R = 140 \ge 10$, $n_R(1-\hat{p}_R) = 210 \ge 10$ (1 pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $\hat{p}_U = 180/400 = 0.45$, $\hat{p}_R = 140/350 = 0.40$. Calculates pooled proportion $\hat{p}_c = \frac{180+140}{400+350} = \frac{320}{750} \approx 0.4267$. Calculates test statistic $z = \frac{(\hat{p}_U - \hat{p}_R) - 0}{\sqrt{\hat{p}_c(1-\hat{p}_c)(\frac{1}{n_U} + \frac{1}{n_R})}} \approx \frac{0.05}{0.0362} \approx 1.38$ (2 pts for calculation and correct value)
  • Part d (2pts): Interprets the p-value: Assuming there is no difference in the true proportions of adults who regularly exercise between urban and rural areas, the probability of observing a sample difference as extreme as $0.05$ (or more extreme) is approximately $0.113$ (1 pt). Since $p\text{-value} = 0.113 > \alpha = 0.10$, the health organization should fail to reject $H_0$. There is not sufficient evidence to conclude that there is a difference in the proportion of adults who regularly exercise between urban and rural areas (1 pt).

FRQ 5

A company manufactures computer chips and claims that no more than $2\%$ of its chips are defective. A consumer group plans to test this claim by taking a random sample of chips. The company's claim leads to the null hypothesis $H_0: p = 0.02$ and the consumer group's suspicion would lead to an alternative hypothesis $H_a: p > 0.02$.
a. Describe a Type I error in the context of this situation. Explain a potential consequence of making this error.
b. Describe a Type II error in the context of this situation. Explain a potential consequence of making this error.
c. If the consumer group decides to increase the sample size for their test, how would this affect the probability of a Type II error, assuming all other factors remain constant? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): A Type I error occurs if the true proportion of defective chips is $2\%$ or less ($H_0$ is true), but the consumer group concludes that it is greater than $2\%$ (rejects $H_0$) (1 pt); Consequence: The company is falsely accused of having a higher defect rate, potentially harming its reputation or leading to unnecessary production adjustments (1 pt)
  • Part b (2pts): A Type II error occurs if the true proportion of defective chips is greater than $2\%$ ($H_0$ is false), but the consumer group concludes that it is $2\%$ or less (fails to reject $H_0$) (1 pt); Consequence: Defective chips are allowed to reach consumers, leading to customer dissatisfaction, warranty claims, and potential loss of future sales for the company (1 pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Increasing the sample size would decrease the probability of a Type II error (1 pt); Justification: A larger sample size provides more information, leading to a more precise estimate of the true proportion. This reduces the variability of the sampling distribution of $\hat{p}$, making it easier to detect a true difference from the null hypothesis, thus increasing the power of the test and decreasing the probability of a Type II error (1 pt).
