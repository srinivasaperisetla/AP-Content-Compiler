FRQ 1

A candy company claims that its bags of mixed candies contain 30% red, 20% yellow, 20% green, 15% orange, and 15% brown candies. A statistics student suspects the distribution is different and takes a random sample of 200 candies from a newly purchased bag.
ColorRedYellowGreenOrangeBrownTotal
Count7035403025200
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for an appropriate test.
b. Calculate the expected count for yellow candies and verify the conditions for conducting this test.
c. The chi-square test statistic is calculated as $\chi^2 = 7.083$. Calculate the degrees of freedom and the p-value associated with this test statistic. (You may use a chi-square table or calculator output.)
d. Based on the p-value from part (c), what conclusion can the student make at the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level? Justify your conclusion in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States correct null and alternative hypotheses in context ($H_0$: The distribution of candy colors matches the company's claim; $H_a$: The distribution of candy colors does not match the company's claim).
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates expected count for yellow candies ($200 \times 0.20 = 40$). Verifies conditions (random sample, all expected counts $\ge 5$).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates degrees of freedom ($5-1=4$). Determines p-value (e.g., $0.131$ from calculator, or range between $0.10$ and $0.25$ from table for $df=4, \chi^2=7.083$).
  • Part d (1pt): States correct conclusion (fail to reject $H_0$) with justification ($p > \alpha$) and in context (no significant evidence that the distribution differs from the claim).

FRQ 2

Researchers are investigating whether the distribution of preferred learning styles (visual, auditory, kinesthetic) is the same for students in rural, suburban, and urban schools. They survey a random sample of 150 students from each type of school, totaling 450 students.
Learning StyleRuralSuburbanUrbanTotal
Visual455550150
Auditory604050150
Kinesthetic455550150
Total150150150450
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for an appropriate test to address the research question.
b. Identify the appropriate inference method and verify the conditions for conducting this test.
c. Calculate the expected count for students from suburban schools who prefer auditory learning.
d. The test statistic for this study is $\chi^2 = 8.667$ and the p-value is 0.069. Based on these results, what conclusion should the researchers make at the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level? Interpret the p-value in the context of this study.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States correct null and alternative hypotheses in context ($H_0$: The distribution of learning styles is the same for students in rural, suburban, and urban schools; $H_a$: The distribution of learning styles is not the same for students in rural, suburban, and urban schools).
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies Chi-Square Test for Homogeneity and verifies conditions (random samples, all expected counts $\ge 5$).
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates expected count for suburban/auditory: $\frac{(\text{Row Total for Auditory}) \times (\text{Column Total for Suburban})}{\text{Grand Total}} = \frac{150 \times 150}{450} = 50$.
  • Part d (2pts): States correct conclusion (fail to reject $H_0$) with justification ($p > \alpha$). Interprets p-value correctly in context (If there were no difference in learning style distributions, there would be a $0.069$ probability of observing a test statistic as extreme or more extreme than $8.667$).

FRQ 3

A large company wants to determine if there is an association between employee satisfaction (satisfied or not satisfied) and their primary mode of commute (car, public transport, bike/walk). They take a random sample of 300 employees and record their satisfaction level and commute mode.
Commute ModeSatisfiedNot SatisfiedTotal
Car10050150
Public Transport603090
Bike/Walk402060
Total200100300
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for an appropriate test to investigate the company's question.
b. Identify the appropriate inference method and verify the conditions for conducting this test.
c. Calculate the expected count for employees who use public transport and are not satisfied.
d. The calculated chi-square test statistic is $\chi^2 = 0.00$ and the p-value is $1.00$. What conclusion can be drawn from these results at the $\alpha = 0.01$ significance level? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States correct null and alternative hypotheses in context ($H_0$: There is no association between employee satisfaction and commute mode; $H_a$: There is an association between employee satisfaction and commute mode).
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies Chi-Square Test for Independence and verifies conditions (random sample, all expected counts $\ge 5$).
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates expected count for public transport/not satisfied: $\frac{(\text{Row Total for Public Transport}) \times (\text{Column Total for Not Satisfied})}{\text{Grand Total}} = \frac{90 \times 100}{300} = 30$.
  • Part d (2pts): States correct conclusion (fail to reject $H_0$) with justification ($p > \alpha$). States that there is no evidence of an association between employee satisfaction and commute mode in context.

FRQ 4

A marketing firm is designing a study to investigate customer preferences for a new product line. They are considering two different approaches.
a. Scenario 1: The firm wants to know if the distribution of preferences (Preference A, B, C, D) for their new product in a sample matches a pre-determined theoretical distribution based on historical market data. Identify the appropriate type of chi-square test the firm should use for this scenario, and briefly describe the chi-square distribution's general shape and how degrees of freedom affect it.
b. Scenario 2: The firm wants to compare the distribution of preferences (Preference A, B, C, D) between two different age groups (under 30, 30 and over). Identify the appropriate type of chi-square test for this scenario.
c. In general, explain why it is inappropriate to use a chi-square test to justify a claim that there is *no* association between two variables, even if the p-value is very high.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies Chi-Square Goodness-of-Fit Test. Describes chi-square distribution as skewed right and becoming less skewed (more symmetric) with increasing degrees of freedom.
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies Chi-Square Test for Homogeneity.
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that a high p-value (failing to reject $H_0$) means there is insufficient evidence to conclude an association, but it does not prove that no association exists. Lack of evidence of an effect is not evidence of no effect.

FRQ 5

A wildlife biologist is studying the habitat preferences of a certain species of bird in a national park. The park has three main habitat types: forest, wetland, and grassland. The biologist suspects that the birds do not use these habitats in equal proportions. To investigate, she tags 180 birds and observes their primary habitat during a specific period.
Habitat TypeForestWetlandGrasslandTotal
Number of Birds806040180
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for the biologist's investigation.
b. Calculate the expected counts for each habitat type, assuming the birds use the habitats in equal proportions.
c. The calculated chi-square test statistic is $\chi^2 = 13.333$ with 2 degrees of freedom. Determine the p-value for this test statistic. (You may use a chi-square table or calculator output.)
d. Based on the results, what conclusion should the biologist draw about the bird's habitat preferences at the $\alpha = 0.01$ significance level? Justify your answer and suggest a specific habitat type that might be preferred or under-utilized compared to the equal proportion expectation.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States correct null and alternative hypotheses in context ($H_0: p_{Forest} = p_{Wetland} = p_{Grassland} = 1/3$; $H_a$: At least one of the habitat proportions is different from $1/3$).
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates expected counts for all three habitats (For 180 birds, $180 \times \frac{1}{3} = 60$ for each habitat).
  • Part c (1pt): Determines the p-value (e.g., $0.00127$ from calculator, or range between $0.001$ and $0.005$ from table for $df=2, \chi^2=13.333$).
  • Part d (2pts): States correct conclusion (reject $H_0$) with justification ($p < \alpha$) and in context (There is significant evidence that the birds do not use the habitats in equal proportions). Suggests a specific habitat type that is preferred (Forest: $80$ observed vs. $60$ expected) or under-utilized (Grassland: $40$ observed vs. $60$ expected).
