AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 5)

FRQ 1

A local town council wants to understand residents' opinions on building a new community center. They decide to survey 100 residents.
a. Describe a method for selecting a simple random sample (SRS) of 100 residents from the town's population list.
b. Explain one potential source of bias if the council decides to only survey residents who attend the next town hall meeting.
c. Suggest an improvement to the sampling method described in part (b) that would help reduce the identified bias.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies using a random number generator/table AND explicitly describes how to assign numbers and select 100 unique residents.
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies a specific type of bias (e.g., volunteer response, undercoverage) AND explains how it would affect the results in context.
  • Part c (1pt): Suggests a valid improvement (e.g., using a different sampling method, outreach to non-attendees) that addresses the bias identified in part (b).

FRQ 2

A researcher wants to investigate the relationship between daily coffee consumption and sleep quality among adults aged 30-50. They recruit 500 adults, ask them to report their average daily coffee intake and rate their sleep quality on a scale of 1 to 10.
a. Identify the type of study described. Justify your answer.
b. Based on this study, can the researcher conclude that increasing coffee consumption causes a decrease in sleep quality? Explain why or why not.
c. Describe the population to which the results of this study can be generalized.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the study as an observational study AND justifies by stating no treatments were imposed/participants were not randomly assigned.
  • Part b (2pts): States that cause-and-effect cannot be concluded AND explains that confounding variables might be present (e.g., stress, other health factors, diet) that could affect both coffee consumption and sleep quality.
  • Part c (1pt): Describes the population as adults aged 30-50 from which the sample was drawn, acknowledging potential limitations of convenience/volunteer sampling if implied.

FRQ 3

A company develops a new type of feed supplement for dairy cows, claiming it increases milk production. They have 60 dairy cows available for the study.
a. Identify the experimental units, the treatment(s), and the response variable in this study.
b. Describe how the company could incorporate a control group into their experiment, and explain the purpose of including a control group.
c. Describe how the company should use randomization in assigning the 60 cows to the different groups.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly identifies the 60 cows as experimental units, the new feed supplement (and potentially no supplement/old supplement) as the treatment(s), and milk production (e.g., liters/day) as the response variable.
  • Part b (1pt): Describes a control group receiving no supplement or a placebo/standard feed AND explains its purpose (e.g., for comparison, to account for extraneous variables).
  • Part c (1pt): Describes a valid randomization method (e.g., using a random number generator to assign cows to groups) to ensure groups are similar on average.

FRQ 4

A university wants to compare the effectiveness of two different online learning platforms, Platform A and Platform B, for teaching introductory calculus. They have 100 students enrolled in introductory calculus and 5 experienced instructors.
a. Describe how to design a completely randomized experiment to compare the two platforms, assigning 50 students to each platform.
b. Describe how to design a matched-pairs experiment to compare the two platforms, creating pairs of students based on their prior math aptitude scores.
c. Compare the strengths of the matched-pairs design from part (b) over the completely randomized design from part (a) for this specific study.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Describes a method for random assignment of students to Platform A or B (e.g., label students 1-100, use RNG to select 50 for A, rest for B) AND ensures all students are assigned.
  • Part b (2pts): Describes matching students based on aptitude scores to form 50 pairs AND randomly assigning one student from each pair to Platform A and the other to Platform B.
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that the matched-pairs design accounts for variability due to prior math aptitude, making the comparison between platforms more precise/reducing confounding by student ability.

FRQ 5

A pharmaceutical company conducts a double-blind, placebo-controlled experiment to test a new pain medication. 200 patients suffering from chronic back pain are randomly assigned to either receive the new medication or a placebo. After 8 weeks, the patients report their pain levels on a scale of 0 to 10 (0 = no pain, 10 = worst pain imaginable). The mean decrease in pain for the medication group was 3.5 points, while for the placebo group it was 1.2 points. A statistical test indicated the difference was statistically significant ($p < 0.01$).
a. Interpret the meaning of the statistically significant result in the context of this study.
b. Explain why it was important for this experiment to be double-blind and placebo-controlled.
c. Identify one potential ethical concern regarding the placebo group in this study, and suggest a way to address it while maintaining experimental validity.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Interprets the $p$-value result, stating that there is strong evidence the new medication is more effective than the placebo in reducing chronic back pain for patients similar to those in the study.
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that double-blinding prevents bias from patient expectations (placebo effect) and researcher influence, and placebo control provides a baseline for comparison, isolating the treatment effect.
  • Part c (2pts): Identifies a concern (e.g., withholding active treatment from patients in pain for an extended period) AND suggests a valid ethical adjustment (e.g., offering the medication to the placebo group after the study, using standard care as a control instead of a pure placebo).
