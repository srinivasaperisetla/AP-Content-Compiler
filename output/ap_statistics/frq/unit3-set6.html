AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 6)

FRQ 1

A researcher wants to investigate if consuming organic vegetables is associated with a lower risk of heart disease in adults. They recruit 1,000 adults and collect data on their typical dietary habits (including organic vegetable consumption) and their medical history regarding heart disease.
a. Identify the primary research question this study aims to answer regarding data collection methods.
b. Identify the type of study described.
c. Explain why this type of study is appropriate for investigating the association between organic vegetable consumption and heart disease risk.
d. Based on this study, can the researchers conclude that consuming organic vegetables causes a lower risk of heart disease? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies a valid question, e.g., "Is there an association between consuming organic vegetables and the risk of heart disease in adults?" or "Do adults who consume organic vegetables have a lower risk of heart disease?".
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies the study as an observational study.
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that it's appropriate because it allows for studying existing habits/conditions without intervention, or because it's unethical/ impractical to assign diets experimentally.
  • Part d (2pts): States no, and justifies that observational studies can only show association, not causation, due to potential confounding variables.

FRQ 2

A school principal wants to survey students' opinions on a new bell schedule. There are 1,500 students in the school, distributed across grades 9-12.
a. Describe how the principal could use simple random sampling to select 150 students for the survey.
b. Describe how the principal could use stratified random sampling to select 150 students, ensuring representation from each grade level.
c. Explain why stratified random sampling might be more appropriate than simple random sampling in this situation.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes assigning a unique number to each student and using a random number generator to select 150 students (or equivalent method).
  • Part b (2pts): Describes dividing students into strata by grade (9, 10, 11, 12) and then selecting a proportional random sample from each stratum (or equal number if total N is not specified for each stratum).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that stratified random sampling ensures representation from each grade level, which might have different opinions on the bell schedule, reducing variability in estimates.

FRQ 3

A town council wants to gauge public opinion on a proposed new public park. They decide to conduct a survey by publishing a questionnaire in the local newspaper and asking interested residents to mail back their responses.
a. Identify one potential source of bias in this sampling method.
b. Explain how this source of bias might affect the results of the survey.
c. Suggest an alternative sampling method that would be more likely to produce a representative sample of residents' opinions.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies voluntary response bias or undercoverage (e.g., people who don't read the paper, or lack of access).
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that people with strong opinions (either positive or negative) are more likely to respond, or that certain demographics are underrepresented, leading to an overestimation/underestimation of support for the park.
  • Part c (2pts): Suggests an appropriate random sampling method, such as simple random sampling of registered voters, systematic sampling of households, or a stratified sample based on geographic regions.

FRQ 4

A pharmaceutical company is testing a new drug designed to lower cholesterol. They recruit 200 volunteers with high cholesterol and randomly assign them to one of two groups. Group A receives the new drug, and Group B receives a placebo. After 3 months, the cholesterol levels of all participants are measured and compared.
a. Identify the experimental units in this study.
b. Identify the explanatory variable and its treatments.
c. Identify the response variable.
d. Explain the purpose of using a placebo in this experiment.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the 200 volunteers (or participants/patients) as the experimental units.
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies the explanatory variable as the type of treatment/drug received, with treatments being the new drug and the placebo.
  • Part c (1pt): Identifies the response variable as the change in cholesterol levels (or final cholesterol levels).
  • Part d (1pt): Explains that a placebo helps to control for the placebo effect, ensuring that observed changes are due to the drug itself and not psychological factors or natural improvement.

FRQ 5

A researcher wants to compare the effectiveness of two different fertilizers (Fertilizer A and Fertilizer B) on the growth of a specific type of plant. They have 60 identical plant seedlings and 30 pots available for the experiment.
a. Describe a completely randomized design for this experiment. Include how treatments would be assigned and explain how confounding variables would be addressed.
b. Propose an improvement to the design described in part (a) if the researcher suspects that the location of the pots in the greenhouse (e.g., closer to a window vs. further) might affect plant growth.
c. Explain how your proposed design in part (b) addresses the potential confounding variable of pot location.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Describes randomly assigning 30 plants to Fertilizer A and 30 plants to Fertilizer B (or similar equal split) and applying the respective fertilizers. Explains that randomization helps balance out unknown confounding variables across groups.
  • Part b (2pts): Proposes a block design where plants are grouped into blocks based on location (e.g., 'window-side' vs. 'interior'). Within each block, plants are randomly assigned to Fertilizer A or Fertilizer B.
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that blocking by location ensures that both fertilizers are tested under similar light/temperature conditions, isolating the effect of the fertilizer itself within each block.
