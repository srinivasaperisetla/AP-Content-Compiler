FRQ 2

A nutritionist is investigating the relationship between the amount of sugar (in grams) in a breakfast cereal and the number of calories per serving. They collect data from a sample of 15 different cereals. A scatterplot shows a positive, roughly linear association. The nutritionist calculates the correlation coefficient to be $r = 0.78$.
a. Interpret the value of the correlation coefficient in the context of this study.
b. If the amount of sugar was measured in teaspoons instead of grams (1 gram $\approx$ 0.25 teaspoons), how would the correlation coefficient change? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Explains that there is a strong, positive linear association between the amount of sugar and the number of calories per serving (1pt); States that larger values of sugar tend to be associated with larger values of calories (1pt)
  • Part b (2pts): States that the correlation coefficient would not change (1pt); Justifies that correlation is a standardized measure and is not affected by changes in the units of measurement for the variables (1pt)

FRQ 3

A company investigates the relationship between the number of hours an employee spends in training ($x$) and their weekly sales ($y$, in thousands of dollars). The least squares regression line based on data from 50 employees is given by $\hat{y} = 2.5 + 0.75x$. The coefficient of determination is $r^2 = 0.64$.
a. Interpret the slope of the least-squares regression line in the context of this problem.
b. Predict the weekly sales for an employee who spent 10 hours in training.
c. Interpret the value of $r^2$ in the context of this problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Explains that for each additional hour of training, the predicted weekly sales increase by $\$0.75$ thousand (or $\$750$) (1pt); References both variables and context (1pt)
  • Part b (1pt): Shows calculation $\hat{y} = 2.5 + 0.75(10) = 10$ and states the predicted sales are $\$10$ thousand (1pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Explains that $64\%$ of the variation in weekly sales can be explained by the linear relationship with training hours (1pt); Mentions context of variables (1pt)

FRQ 4

A school administrator collected data on the number of hours students spent studying for a final exam and their scores on the exam. The scatterplot below shows the relationship, and a linear model was fit to the data. The residual plot is also shown below.
a. Using the residual plot, determine if a linear model is appropriate for these data. Justify your answer.
b. One student studied for 6 hours and scored 80 on the exam. The predicted score for a student who studied 6 hours was 75. Calculate the residual for this student and interpret its meaning in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): States that a linear model is not appropriate (1pt); Justifies by explaining that there is a clear pattern (e.g., curved pattern, U-shape) in the residual plot, indicating that a linear model is not the best fit (1pt)
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates residual as $80 - 75 = 5$ (1pt); Interprets that this student's actual score was 5 points higher than what the linear model predicted (1pt)

FRQ 5

A marine biologist is studying the relationship between the length ($L$, in cm) and mass ($M$, in grams) of a certain species of fish. A scatterplot of $M$ versus $L$ shows a strong positive, non-linear relationship. To achieve linearity, the biologist performs a transformation, analyzing $\log(M)$ versus $\log(L)$. The least-squares regression line for the transformed data is $\widehat{\log(M)} = 0.52 + 2.8 \log(L)$.
a. Describe the characteristics of an influential point in a scatterplot.
b. Explain why the biologist chose to transform both variables using a logarithm in this context.
c. Predict the mass (in grams) of a fish with a length of $10$ cm using the transformed model. Show your work.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Explains that an influential point is a data point that, if removed, would significantly change the slope and/or y-intercept of the least-squares regression line (1pt); May also mention it is often an outlier in the x-direction (1pt)
  • Part b (2pts): Explains that transforming both variables (e.g., $\log(y)$ vs. $\log(x)$) is often used when the original relationship is non-linear and exhibits increasing variation as $x$ increases, or to linearize a power relationship (1pt); Notes that using logarithms helps to linearize relationships that are multiplicative or exponential (1pt)
  • Part c (2pts): Substitutes $L=10$ into the transformed equation: $\widehat{\log(M)} = 0.52 + 2.8 \log(10) = 0.52 + 2.8(1) = 3.32$ (1pt); Transforms back to original scale: $M = 10^{3.32} \approx 2089.29$ grams (1pt)
