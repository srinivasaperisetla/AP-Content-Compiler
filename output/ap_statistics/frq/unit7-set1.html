(Since $0.0001 < 0.01$, reject $H_0$. There is strong evidence that the training regimen improves vertical jump height.)

FRQ 3

A marine biologist wants to estimate the mean length of a certain species of fish in a large lake. A random sample of $n=30$ fish of this species is caught, and their lengths (in cm) are measured. The sample mean length is $\bar{x} = 24.3$ cm, and the sample standard deviation is $s = 2.7$ cm.
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure, and verify the conditions for its use.
b. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true mean length of this species of fish.
c. Interpret the confidence interval from part (b) in the context of this problem.
d. Based on your interval, does it provide convincing evidence that the true mean length of this fish species is greater than 23 cm? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly identifies one-sample t-interval for a mean (1pt) and verifies random sample, $n<10\%$ of population, and approximately normal population distribution (or $n \ge 30$) (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Correctly calculates the interval using $df=29$ and $t^* \approx 2.045$ as $24.3 \pm 2.045 \left(\frac{2.7}{\sqrt{30}}\right) = (23.3, 25.3)$ (2pts).
  • Part c (1pt): Provides a correct interpretation of the interval in context (e.g., "We are 95% confident that the true mean length... is between 23.3 cm and 25.3 cm").
  • Part d (1pt): States that there is convincing evidence because the entire interval is above 23 cm (1pt).

FRQ 4

A car manufacturer claims that a new model achieves an average highway fuel efficiency of 35 miles per gallon (mpg). An environmental agency wants to test this claim, suspecting the true average is lower. They select a random sample of $n=40$ cars of this new model and measure their highway fuel efficiency. The sample mean is $\bar{x} = 34.2$ mpg with a standard deviation of $s = 2.5$ mpg.
a. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses for this test.
b. Verify the conditions for performing this significance test.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Based on your results, what conclusion should the environmental agency make about the manufacturer's claim? Use a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): $H_0: \mu = 35$ mpg and $H_a: \mu < 35$ mpg (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Verifies random sample (given) (1pt), $n<10\%$ of population (assume large production) (0.5pt), and $n \ge 30$ for approximate normality (given $n=40$) (0.5pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates test statistic $t = \frac{34.2 - 35}{2.5/\sqrt{40}} \approx -2.02$ (1pt) and finds p-value $P(t_{39} < -2.02) \approx 0.024$ (1pt).
  • Part d (1pt): Compares p-value to $\alpha$ ($0.024 < 0.05$) and rejects $H_0$. Concludes there is convincing evidence that the true mean fuel efficiency is less than 35 mpg (1pt).

FRQ 5

A health clinic implements a new 12-week weight-loss program. To assess its effectiveness, they enroll $n=15$ volunteers and record their weight (in pounds) before starting the program and after completing it. The differences in weight (After - Before) are calculated for each participant. The mean difference is $\bar{x}_d = -5.8$ pounds with a standard deviation of $s_d = 4.1$ pounds. A dot plot of the differences shows no strong skewness or outliers.
a. Identify the appropriate inference procedure to determine if the program is effective, and state the null and alternative hypotheses.
b. Verify the conditions for performing this significance test.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. At the $\alpha = 0.01$ significance level, what conclusion should the clinic make regarding the effectiveness of the weight-loss program?

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies paired t-test for a mean difference (1pt) and states $H_0: \mu_d = 0$ versus $H_a: \mu_d < 0$, where $\mu_d$ is the true mean difference (After - Before) (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Verifies random assignment/selection (assume participants are representative or randomly selected, or state this is a limitation) (0.5pt), $n<10\%$ of population (assume large population of potential participants) (0.5pt), and approximately normal distribution of differences (stated dot plot shows no strong skewness/outliers) (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates test statistic $t = \frac{-5.8 - 0}{4.1/\sqrt{15}} \approx -5.48$ (1pt) and finds p-value $P(t_{14} < -5.48) \approx 0.000$ (very small) (1pt).
  • Part d (1pt): Compares p-value to $\alpha$ (p-value $< 0.01$) and rejects $H_0$. Concludes there is convincing evidence that the mean weight loss is greater than 0, meaning the program is effective (1pt).
