AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 16)

FRQ 1

A high school principal wants to gauge student opinion on the quality of school lunch. She decides to send an email survey to all students and uses the first 100 responses received.
a. Identify the sampling method used by the principal.
b. Explain why this sampling method is NOT appropriate for gathering representative data about student opinion.
c. Identify one potential source of bias in this sampling method and explain how it might affect the results.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the sampling method as convenience sampling or voluntary response sampling.
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that the sample is not representative of the student population because it relies on self-selection or ease of access, leading to undercoverage or overrepresentation of certain groups.
  • Part c (2pts): Identifies a source of bias (e.g., voluntary response bias, nonresponse bias, undercoverage) and explains how it would lead to an over- or underestimation of lunch quality (e.g., only students with strong opinions respond, resulting in more negative/positive feedback than the overall student body).

FRQ 2

Researchers are interested in whether regular exercise reduces the risk of heart disease. They recruit 1,000 adults and collect data on their exercise habits, diet, and medical history over 10 years. At the end of the study, they compare the incidence of heart disease between those who exercised regularly and those who did not.
a. Identify the type of study described.
b. Can the researchers conclude that regular exercise causes a reduction in heart disease risk? Justify your answer.
c. Describe how the researchers could design an experiment to investigate if regular exercise causes a reduction in heart disease risk.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the study as an observational study.
  • Part b (2pts): States that cause-and-effect cannot be concluded because it is an observational study and provides a correct justification (e.g., confounding variables cannot be controlled, no random assignment).
  • Part c (2pts): Describes a basic experimental design including random assignment of participants to at least two groups (e.g., exercise group, control group) and measuring the outcome (heart disease risk).

FRQ 3

A pharmaceutical company developed a new drug to lower blood pressure. They want to test its effectiveness compared to a placebo. They recruit 200 volunteers with high blood pressure and plan to administer the new drug to half of them and a placebo to the other half for three months. They will measure blood pressure before and after the treatment period.
a. Identify the experimental units and the treatments in this study.
b. Describe how the researchers should use blinding in this experiment.
c. Explain the purpose of including a placebo in this experimental design.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies experimental units as the 200 volunteers and treatments as the new drug and the placebo.
  • Part b (2pts): Describes that participants should be unaware of which treatment they receive (single blind) and, ideally, that those administering the drug/measuring outcomes should also be unaware (double blind).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that the placebo serves as a control to account for the placebo effect, allowing researchers to isolate the true effect of the new drug.

FRQ 4

A food scientist wants to compare the effectiveness of two different food preservation methods (Method A and Method B) on extending the shelf life of a new type of fruit. They have 200 fruits available for the study.
a. Describe a completely randomized design for this experiment.
b. Describe a matched-pairs design for this experiment. Explain why a matched-pairs design might be more appropriate than a completely randomized design in this context.
c. Suppose the scientists use a completely randomized design and find that Method A results in a significantly longer shelf life ($p < 0.01$) compared to Method B. Interpret this result in the context of the study.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes random assignment of 100 fruits to Method A and 100 fruits to Method B, followed by shelf life measurement for each fruit.
  • Part b (2pts): Describes matching fruits based on a characteristic (e.g., ripeness, size) into 100 pairs, randomly assigning Method A to one fruit in each pair and Method B to the other, then comparing within pairs. Explains that matched pairs reduce variability due to confounding factors, making it easier to detect a difference between methods.
  • Part c (1pt): Interprets $p < 0.01$ as meaning there is strong evidence that Method A causes a longer shelf life for this type of fruit than Method B, and this difference is unlikely to occur by random chance if there were no actual difference.

FRQ 5

A university housing office wants to estimate the proportion of students who are satisfied with their on-campus housing. There are 10,000 students living in 10 different dormitories on campus.
a. Describe a method for selecting a simple random sample (SRS) of 500 students from the 10,000 students living in campus housing. Assume you have a complete list of all students living in campus housing.
b. Describe an advantage of using a stratified random sample, rather than an SRS, to estimate the proportion of satisfied students. Explain how you would implement a stratified random sample in this context.
c. Suppose the housing office sends an email survey to all 10,000 students. Identify and describe one potential source of bias that could arise from this data collection method.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Assigns a unique numerical label to each of the 10,000 students and uses a random number generator to select 500 distinct labels.
  • Part b (2pts): Describes that stratified random sampling ensures representation from each dormitory (stratum), potentially reducing variability in the estimate. Explains implementation by sampling a proportional number of students from each of the 10 dormitories (strata).
  • Part c (2pts): Identifies voluntary response bias or nonresponse bias and describes how it would affect the estimate (e.g., only very satisfied or very dissatisfied students might respond, leading to an overestimation or underestimation of overall satisfaction).
