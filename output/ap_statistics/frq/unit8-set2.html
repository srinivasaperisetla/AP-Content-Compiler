FRQ 1

A candy company claims that its bags of multi-colored candies contain 30% red, 20% blue, 20% green, 15% yellow, and 15% orange candies. A statistics student suspects the proportions are different and collects a random sample of 200 candies from a large bag. The observed counts are given in the table below.
ColorObserved Count
Red55
Blue35
Green30
Yellow40
Orange40
a. State the appropriate null and alternative hypotheses for a test to determine if the company's stated proportions are correct.
b. Identify the appropriate inference method and verify the conditions for this test.
c. Calculate the expected count for green candies. Then, state the degrees of freedom for this chi-square test.
d. Given the calculated chi-square test statistic is $\chi^2 = 8.76$ with a p-value of 0.067, interpret the p-value in the context of this study. Justify whether the student has convincing evidence at the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level that the company's stated proportions are incorrect.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States correct null and alternative hypotheses, including parameters in context.
  • Part b (2pts): Identifies chi-square goodness-of-fit test. Verifies the Random condition (random sample stated). Verifies the Large Counts condition (all expected counts $\ge 5$).
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates expected count for green candies correctly ($40$). States degrees of freedom correctly ($5-1=4$).
  • Part d (2pts): Interprets the p-value correctly in context. Makes a correct decision based on the p-value and $\alpha$ level. Provides a correct justification for the decision in context.

FRQ 5

A school administrator claims that the distribution of student enrollment across the four grade levels (9th, 10th, 11th, 12th) is uniform. To test this claim, a random sample of 400 students was selected, and their grade level was recorded. A chi-square goodness-of-fit test was performed, yielding a chi-square test statistic of $\chi^2 = 12.8$ with 3 degrees of freedom and a p-value of 0.0051. The observed and expected counts are shown below.
Grade LevelObserved CountExpected Count
9th120100
10th110100
11th80100
12th90100
Total400400
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for this test. Based on the p-value and a significance level of $\alpha = 0.01$, what conclusion can be drawn? Justify your answer.
b. For each grade level, calculate the chi-square component $(observed - expected)^2/expected$. Identify which grade level contributes most to the calculated chi-square test statistic. Explain what this large contribution suggests.
c. The observed counts for 9th and 10th grade were 120 and 110, respectively. What questions might be suggested by the variation between these observed and their expected counts, given the overall conclusion of the test?
d. Suppose the sample of 400 students was not a random sample, but instead consisted of all students present on a particular day when attendance was unusually low among 11th and 12th graders due to a field trip. Explain how this would affect the validity of the conclusions drawn from the test.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): States correct null and alternative hypotheses (uniform distribution of students across grades). Makes a correct decision (reject $H_0$) based on p-value and $\alpha$. Provides correct justification that there is convincing evidence that the distribution is not uniform.
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates chi-square component for each grade level: 9th: $\frac{(120-100)^2}{100} = 4$; 10th: $\frac{(110-100)^2}{100} = 1$; 11th: $\frac{(80-100)^2}{100} = 4$; 12th: $\frac{(90-100)^2}{100} = 1$. Identifies 9th and 11th grades as contributing most. Explains that these grades deviate most from their expected counts, suggesting disproportionate enrollment compared to a uniform distribution.
  • Part c (1pt): Formulates questions regarding the specific reason for the observed deviation (e.g., "Why are there significantly more 9th graders and significantly fewer 11th graders than expected?", "Is there an underlying reason for the enrollment discrepancies in these grades?").
  • Part d (1pt): Explains that the non-random sample (and bias) violates the inference conditions, making the conclusions invalid or not generalizable to the entire student population. The sample is not representative.
