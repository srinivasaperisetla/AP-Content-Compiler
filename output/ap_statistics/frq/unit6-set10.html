AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 6: Inference for Categorical Data: Proportions (Set 10)

FRQ 1

A company manufactures computer chips. In a random sample of 250 chips, 20 were found to be defective. The company wants to estimate the true proportion of defective chips with 95% confidence.
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure and verify the conditions for its use.
b. Calculate a 95% confidence interval for the true proportion of defective chips.
c. Interpret the calculated confidence interval in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly identifies a one-sample z-interval for proportions (1pt) and verifies conditions for random sample, independence (10% condition), and Normality (large counts) (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Shows correct calculation for sample proportion, standard error, z*-critical value, and calculates the interval correctly (1pt for formula/setup, 1pt for correct interval).
  • Part c (1pt): Provides a correct interpretation of the confidence interval in the context of defective chips and 95% confidence.

FRQ 2

A politician claims that more than 60% of voters in her district support a new policy. A random sample of 400 voters was surveyed, and 250 of them supported the policy. Perform a significance test to evaluate the politician's claim.
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses, and identify the appropriate inference method.
b. Verify the conditions for conducting this significance test.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Based on your p-value, what conclusion can be drawn about the politician's claim at a significance level of α = 0.05? Justify your conclusion.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly states H0: p=0.60 and Ha: p>0.60 (1pt) and identifies a one-sample z-test for proportions (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Verifies conditions for random sample, independence (10% condition), and Normality (large counts) (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Correctly calculates the test statistic (z-score) (1pt) and the p-value (1pt).
  • Part d (2pts): States a correct conclusion in context (either reject H0 or fail to reject H0) (1pt) and provides a justification linking the p-value to α (1pt).

FRQ 3

A medical researcher is testing a new drug for a certain disease. The null hypothesis is that the drug has no effect (p = proportion cured is the same as placebo), and the alternative hypothesis is that the drug is effective (p > proportion cured for placebo).
a. Describe what a Type I error would be in this context.
b. Describe what a Type II error would be in this context.
c. Explain a consequence of making a Type I error in this situation.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly describes a Type I error (rejecting H0 when H0 is true) in the context of the drug (concluding the drug is effective when it is not).
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly describes a Type II error (failing to reject H0 when H0 is false) in the context of the drug (concluding the drug is not effective when it actually is).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains a relevant consequence of a Type I error, such as prescribing an ineffective drug, wasting resources, or potential patient harm from an unhelpful drug.

FRQ 4

A marketing team wants to compare the effectiveness of two different advertisement campaigns (Campaign A and Campaign B) for a new product. Campaign A was shown to a random sample of 300 potential customers, and 120 of them expressed interest in the product. Campaign B was shown to a separate random sample of 250 potential customers, and 80 of them expressed interest. The team wants to construct a 90% confidence interval for the difference in the true proportions of potential customers who express interest.
a. Identify the appropriate confidence interval procedure and verify the necessary conditions for its use.
b. Calculate a 90% confidence interval for the difference in the true proportions of potential customers who express interest (p_A - p_B).
c. Interpret the confidence interval you calculated in part (b) in the context of the problem.
d. Based on your confidence interval, can the marketing team conclude that there is a significant difference in the effectiveness of the two campaigns? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly identifies a two-sample z-interval for a difference in proportions (1pt) and verifies conditions for two independent random samples, independence (10% condition for both samples), and Normality (large counts for all four expected successes/failures) (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Shows correct calculation for sample proportions, unpooled standard error, z*-critical value, and calculates the interval correctly (1pt for formula/setup, 1pt for correct interval).
  • Part c (1pt): Provides a correct interpretation of the confidence interval for the difference in proportions in context.
  • Part d (1pt): States a correct conclusion about a significant difference (or lack thereof) based on whether 0 is included in the interval, and provides justification in context (e.g., since 0 is/is not in the interval, we do/do not have convincing evidence).

FRQ 5

A company claims that a new training program increases employee satisfaction. Before the program, a random sample of 150 employees showed that 75 were satisfied with their job. After the program, a separate random sample of 160 employees showed that 96 were satisfied. Conduct a significance test to determine if there is convincing evidence that the training program increased employee satisfaction at the α = 0.01 significance level.
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses, defining any parameters you use, and identify the appropriate inference method.
b. Verify the conditions for performing this significance test.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Based on your significance test, what conclusion can be made at the α = 0.01 level? Justify your conclusion in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Defines parameters p_before and p_after (1pt), correctly states H0: p_before = p_after and Ha: p_before < p_after (or equivalent p_after > p_before) (1pt for hypotheses), and identifies a two-sample z-test for a difference in proportions (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Verifies conditions for two independent random samples, independence (10% condition for both samples), and Normality (large counts for pooled successes/failures) (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Correctly calculates the pooled proportion, test statistic (z-score), and the p-value (1pt for pooled proportion/test statistic, 1pt for p-value).
  • Part d (2pts): States a correct conclusion in context (reject H0 or fail to reject H0) (1pt) and provides a justification linking the p-value to α (1pt).
