AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 4: Probability, Random Variables, and Probability Distributions (Set 14)

FRQ 1

A small startup company produces smart sensors. Due to the complex manufacturing process, not all sensors pass the final quality check. The number of defective sensors in a randomly selected batch of 50 sensors is a discrete random variable, $X$. The probability distribution for $X$ is given in the table below.
Number of Defective Sensors ($x$)Probability $P(X=x)$
00.10
10.25
20.35
30.20
40.10
a. Verify that the given table represents a valid probability distribution.
b. Calculate the expected number of defective sensors in a batch.
c. Calculate the standard deviation of the number of defective sensors in a batch.
d. Interpret the expected value calculated in part (b) in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Shows that all probabilities are between 0 and 1, inclusive, and sum to 1 (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $E(X) = \sum x_i P(X=x_i)$ (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $Var(X) = \sum (x_i - \mu_X)^2 P(X=x_i)$ (1pt)
  • Calculates $SD(X) = \sqrt{Var(X)}$ (1pt).
  • Part d (1pt): Interprets $E(X)$ as the long-run average number of defective sensors per batch (1pt).

FRQ 2

A company produces chocolate eggs, and historically, 15% of these eggs contain a golden ticket. To simulate the production process, a student uses a random number generator. Numbers from 01 to 15 represent an egg with a golden ticket, and numbers from 16 to 99, along with 00, represent an egg without a golden ticket. The student runs 50 trials, each representing one egg, and finds that 9 of them contained a golden ticket.
a. Based on the student's simulation, what is the estimated probability that a randomly selected chocolate egg contains a golden ticket?
b. Based on the historical data, what is the theoretical probability that a randomly selected chocolate egg contains a golden ticket?
c. Explain why the estimated probability from the simulation might be different from the theoretical probability.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly states the estimated probability as $9/50 = 0.18$.
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly states the theoretical probability as $0.15$.
  • Part c (2pts): Explains that simulation results are subject to sampling variability and will not always exactly match theoretical probabilities, especially with a small number of trials.

FRQ 3

A small bakery sells a special cake. The number of cakes sold per day, $X$, varies and has the probability distribution shown in the table below.
Number of Cakes Sold ($x$)Probability $P(X=x)$
00.10
10.25
20.35
3?
40.05
a. Determine the missing probability for $P(X=3)$ in the table. Show your work.
b. Calculate the expected number of cakes sold per day, $E(X)$. Show your work.
c. Interpret the expected value calculated in part (b) in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly calculates $P(X=3) = 1 - (0.10+0.25+0.35+0.05) = 1 - 0.75 = 0.25$.
  • Part b (2pts): Correctly calculates $E(X) = (0 \times 0.10) + (1 \times 0.25) + (2 \times 0.35) + (3 \times 0.25) + (4 \times 0.05) = 0 + 0.25 + 0.70 + 0.75 + 0.20 = 1.9$ cakes. Shows calculation.
  • Part c (1pt): Interprets $E(X)$ as the long-run average number of cakes sold per day if this process were repeated many times.

FRQ 4

A pharmaceutical company claims that a new drug is effective for 80% of patients. A researcher plans to administer the drug to a random sample of 12 patients.
a. Verify that the conditions for a binomial distribution are met for the number of effective treatments in this sample.
b. Calculate the probability that exactly 10 of the 12 patients experience an effective treatment. Show your work.
c. Calculate the expected number of patients who will experience an effective treatment and the standard deviation for the number of effective treatments in this sample.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Verifies all four binomial conditions: (1) Fixed number of trials ($n=12$), (2) Two outcomes (effective/not effective), (3) Independent trials (random sample, one patient's outcome doesn't affect another's), (4) Constant probability of success ($p=0.80$).
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $P(X=10) = \binom{12}{10}(0.80)^{10}(0.20)^2 \approx 0.283$. Work shown for binomial probability formula or calculator notation (e.g., `binompdf(12, 0.80, 10)`).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $E(X) = n p = 12 \times 0.80 = 9.6$ patients (1pt) and $\sigma_X = \sqrt{n p (1-p)} = \sqrt{12 \times 0.80 \times 0.20} = \sqrt{1.92} \approx 1.386$ patients (1pt).

FRQ 5

An archer has a 70% chance of hitting the bullseye on any given shot. Each shot is independent. Let $Y$ be the number of shots until the archer hits the bullseye for the first time.
a. Calculate the probability that the archer hits the bullseye for the first time on the third shot. Show your work.
b. Calculate the expected number of shots needed for the archer to hit the bullseye for the first time. Show your work.
c. Interpret the expected value calculated in part (b) in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Calculates $P(Y=3) = (1-0.70)^2 \times 0.70 = (0.30)^2 \times 0.70 = 0.09 \times 0.70 = 0.063$. Work shown for geometric probability formula or calculator notation (e.g., `geometpdf(0.70, 3)`).
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $E(Y) = \frac{1}{p} = \frac{1}{0.70} \approx 1.4286$ shots. Shows calculation.
  • Part c (1pt): Interprets $E(Y)$ as the long-run average number of shots required to hit the bullseye for the first time, if this process were repeated many times.
