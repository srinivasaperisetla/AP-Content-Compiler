AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 17)

FRQ 1

A large high school wants to estimate the proportion of its 2000 students who plan to attend the upcoming prom. The principal suggests surveying the first 100 students who arrive at school on a specific Monday morning.
a. Identify the sampling method proposed by the principal.
b. Explain whether this sampling method is appropriate for estimating the proportion of students who plan to attend prom. Justify your answer.
c. Describe a different appropriate sampling method that the school could use to get a more representative sample, and explain how to implement it.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly identifies the sampling method as a convenience sample.
  • Part b (2pts): States that the method is not appropriate (1pt) AND provides a clear justification by identifying a source of bias (e.g., students arriving early may not be representative of all students, creating undercoverage or selection bias) (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Describes a simple random sample (SRS) or stratified random sample (1pt) AND explains a valid procedure for implementing it (e.g., assign each student a number and use a random number generator for SRS, or divide by grade level and take a proportional SRS from each grade for stratified) (1pt).

FRQ 2

Researchers are investigating the relationship between daily coffee consumption and academic performance among college students.
a. Identify the type of study described if researchers collect data on students' coffee habits and their GPAs without intervening.
b. Describe how to design a completely randomized experiment to investigate this relationship, including identification of the explanatory and response variables, and a method of randomly assigning students to groups.
c. Explain whether a cause-and-effect conclusion about coffee consumption and academic performance can be drawn from the study described in part (a). Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly identifies the study as an observational study.
  • Part b (3pts): Identifies daily coffee consumption (or treatment level like "number of cups") as the explanatory variable (1pt) AND academic performance (e.g., GPA or test scores) as the response variable (1pt) AND describes a valid method for randomly assigning students to different coffee consumption levels (e.g., assign students to drink 0, 1, or 2+ cups per day) (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): States that a cause-and-effect conclusion cannot be drawn (1pt) AND provides a clear justification, such as the presence of confounding variables (e.g., study habits, major, sleep, time spent working) (1pt).

FRQ 3

A pharmaceutical company develops a new medication for reducing allergy symptoms. They want to test its effectiveness against a placebo. They recruit 300 volunteers who suffer from seasonal allergies.
a. Identify the experimental units, explanatory variable, and response variable in this study.
b. Describe how to incorporate the principles of blinding and randomization in the design of this experiment.
c. Explain why including a placebo group is important in this experiment.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (3pts): Identifies the 300 volunteers as the experimental units (1pt) AND the medication/treatment (or dosage level) as the explanatory variable (1pt) AND the reduction in allergy symptoms (or symptom severity score) as the response variable (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Describes random assignment of volunteers to treatment groups (e.g., using a random number generator or drawing names from a hat to assign approximately 150 to medication and 150 to placebo) (1pt) AND describes double-blinding (neither the volunteers nor the researchers interacting with them know who receives the medication or placebo) (1pt).
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that a placebo group accounts for the placebo effect or provides a baseline for comparison against the active treatment.

FRQ 4

A local city council wants to gauge public opinion on a proposal to build a new community center. They send a detailed online survey to all registered voters in the city. The survey contains several questions about the proposed center, including its costs and potential benefits.
a. Identify one potential source of bias in the city council's proposed survey method.
b. Explain how the source of bias identified in part (a) might affect the results of the survey.
c. Describe a modification to the survey method that would help reduce the bias identified in part (a).

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies a valid source of bias, such as voluntary response bias, nonresponse bias, or undercoverage (for voters without internet access or who don't regularly check email).
  • Part b (1pt): Explains how the identified bias would systematically over- or underestimate the true public opinion (e.g., if voluntary response bias is identified, then only those with strong opinions (likely in favor or strongly against) will respond, potentially exaggerating the perceived support or opposition).
  • Part c (2pts): Proposes a specific, feasible modification to the survey method that would address the identified bias (e.g., for nonresponse/voluntary response, follow-up contacts via mail/phone, or offering incentives; for undercoverage, provide paper surveys or phone calls to a random sample) (1pt) AND explains how this modification would reduce the bias (1pt).

FRQ 5

A company develops two different training programs (Program A and Program B) for its sales team, aiming to increase average monthly sales. They have 100 sales associates available for the study.
a. Describe how to design a completely randomized experiment to compare the effectiveness of the two training programs.
b. Suppose the company instead decided to use a matched-pairs experimental design. Describe how this design would be implemented for this study.
c. Explain one advantage of the matched-pairs design over the completely randomized design in this context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Describes randomly assigning the 100 associates to either Program A or Program B (50 in each group) (1pt) AND explains that sales performance will be measured for each group after the training (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Describes forming 50 pairs of associates based on a relevant characteristic (e.g., prior sales performance, experience level, or region) (1pt) AND within each pair, randomly assigning one associate to Program A and the other to Program B (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Explains that the matched-pairs design reduces variability in the response variable (sales) by controlling for individual differences between associates (or other extraneous variables controlled by pairing) (1pt), thereby increasing the power to detect a difference between programs if one exists (1pt).
