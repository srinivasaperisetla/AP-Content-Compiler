FRQ 1

A survey asked 150 high school students about their favorite type of pet. The results are summarized below by gender.
Pet TypeMale StudentsFemale Students
Dog4530
Cat2035
Fish1510
Bird510
Other1010
a. Construct a relative frequency table for the pet preference distribution for all students combined.
b. Create a comparative bar chart to display the distribution of pet preferences for male and female students.
c. Based on your bar chart, describe one key difference in pet preferences between male and female students.
d. Explain why a pie chart would not be appropriate for comparing pet preferences between male and female students.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly calculates relative frequencies for all categories and presents them in a table.
  • Part b (2pts): Correctly constructs a comparative bar chart with properly labeled axes and categories, showing separate bars for males and females or stacked bars per pet type.
  • Part c (1pt): Identifies a clear difference in preferences (e.g., males prefer dogs more than females, females prefer cats more than males) based on the graph.
  • Part d (1pt): Explains that a pie chart shows parts of a whole for a single group, and cannot effectively compare distributions of two different groups (males and females).

FRQ 2

A small business owner tracked the daily number of customers visiting her store for 15 days. The number of customers each day was: $28, 32, 25, 30, 35, 29, 31, 26, 33, 27, 34, 30, 29, 32, 28$.
a. Calculate the mean and median number of customers.
b. Calculate the interquartile range (IQR) and the standard deviation of the number of customers.
c. Describe the shape, center, and spread of the distribution of daily customer visits.
d. Explain which measures of center and spread would be more appropriate for describing this distribution, and justify your choice.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly calculates the mean ($\bar{x} = 30$) and median (30).
  • Part b (2pts): Correctly calculates IQR ($Q_3-Q_1 = 32.5-28 = 4.5$) and standard deviation ($s \approx 2.92$).
  • Part c (1pt): Describes the shape as roughly symmetric, center around 30, and spread using IQR or standard deviation.
  • Part d (1pt): Explains that mean and standard deviation are more appropriate because the distribution appears symmetric with no apparent outliers.

FRQ 3

GroupData (Hours)
A5, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 12, 13, 15, 20

FRQ 4

The weights of adult male grizzly bears in a certain region are approximately normally distributed with a mean of $\mu = 380$ kg and a standard deviation of $\sigma = 50$ kg.
a. Calculate the z-score for an adult male grizzly bear weighing 450 kg.
b. Determine the proportion of adult male grizzly bears in this region that weigh more than 450 kg.
c. A wildlife biologist finds a bear weighing 300 kg. Compare the relative position of this bear's weight to a bear weighing 450 kg within the distribution of weights.
d. Explain why the normal distribution is often a suitable model for natural phenomena like animal weights.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly calculates the z-score as $z = \frac{450 - 380}{50} = 1.4$.
  • Part b (2pts): Correctly determines the proportion (e.g., using a z-table or calculator, $P(Z > 1.4) \approx 0.0808$).
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates the z-score for 300 kg ($z = \frac{300 - 380}{50} = -1.6$) and compares relative positions (e.g., 300 kg is further below the mean than 450 kg is above it, indicating 300 kg is relatively lighter/more unusual).
  • Part d (1pt): Explains that natural phenomena often result from many small, independent random factors, which tend to average out to a bell-shaped, symmetric distribution.

FRQ 5

A school conducted a survey of its 200 students to gather data on their daily commute to school. For each student, they recorded their primary mode of transportation (e.g., walk, bus, car, bike) and their commute time in minutes.
a. Identify the types of variables collected in this survey (e.g., categorical or quantitative).
b. The commute times (in minutes) for a random sample of 25 students who walk to school are: $5, 7, 8, 8, 9, 10, 10, 10, 11, 12, 12, 13, 14, 15, 15, 16, 17, 18, 18, 19, 20, 22, 25, 28, 30$. Construct a stem-and-leaf plot for this data.
c. Calculate the median and range for the commute times of the walking students. Based on the stem-and-leaf plot, describe the shape of this distribution.
d. Suppose the school wants to compare the distribution of commute times for students who take the bus versus those who ride in a car. Describe an appropriate graphical display for this comparison and explain what specific features you would look for in the display to draw a conclusion.
e. A local transit authority claims that "the typical student commute time in this area is about 15 minutes, with most commutes falling between 10 and 20 minutes." Using the information from part (c) for walking students, evaluate whether this claim appears reasonable for *this specific group* of students.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly identifies "mode of transportation" as categorical and "commute time" as quantitative.
  • Part b (2pts): Constructs a correct stem-and-leaf plot with a key and appropriate stems and leaves.
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates the median (14 minutes) and range (30-5=25 minutes) and describes the shape (e.g., slightly skewed right or roughly symmetric with a slight tail to the right).
  • Part d (1pt): Describes using parallel boxplots or comparative dot plots/histograms and explains looking for differences in center (median/mean), spread (IQR/range), and shape (skewness, outliers).
  • Part e (1pt): Evaluates the claim by comparing the walking students' median and typical range (e.g., IQR) to the claim's values. For walking students, a median of 14 minutes is close to 15, and an IQR from say 10 to 19 minutes is consistent with "most commutes falling between 10 and 20 minutes". So, the claim appears reasonable for walking students.
