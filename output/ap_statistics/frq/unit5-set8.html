AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 5: Sampling Distributions (Set 8)

FRQ 1

A company claims that 70% of its online customers are satisfied with their recent purchase. A consumer advocacy group suspects this claim is too high. To investigate, they plan to take a random sample of 150 online customers.
a. Describe the sampling distribution of the sample proportion of satisfied customers, $\hat{p}$, assuming the company's claim is true. Be sure to calculate the mean and standard deviation.
b. Justify whether the sampling distribution of $\hat{p}$ can be approximated by a normal distribution.
c. If the company's claim is true, calculate the probability that in a random sample of 150 customers, the sample proportion of satisfied customers is less than 0.65.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Calculates mean of $\hat{p}$ as $p=0.70$ (1pt); Calculates standard deviation of $\hat{p}$ as $\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.70(0.30)}{150}}$ (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Checks $np \ge 10$ and $n(1-p) \ge 10$ conditions ($150 \times 0.70 = 105 \ge 10$ and $150 \times 0.30 = 45 \ge 10$) and concludes approximately normal (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates the $z$-score correctly, $z = \frac{0.65 - 0.70}{\sqrt{\frac{0.70(0.30)}{150}}}$ (1pt); Calculates the correct probability $P(\hat{p} < 0.65) \approx 0.093$ (1pt).

FRQ 2

The weight of a certain type of fruit grown on a farm is known to have a mean of $\mu = 180$ grams and a standard deviation of $\sigma = 25$ grams. The distribution of individual fruit weights is unknown. A random sample of 40 fruits is selected for a grocery store.
a. Describe the sampling distribution of the sample mean weight, $\bar{x}$, for random samples of 40 fruits. Be sure to calculate the mean and standard deviation.
b. Explain why it is appropriate to use a normal distribution to model the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$, even though the population distribution of individual fruit weights is unknown.
c. Calculate the probability that the sample mean weight for a random sample of 40 fruits is greater than 190 grams.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies the mean of $\bar{x}$ as $\mu = 180$ grams (1pt); Calculates the standard deviation of $\bar{x}$ as $\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{25}{\sqrt{40}}$ (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): States that because the sample size $n=40$ is large (typically $n \ge 30$), the Central Limit Theorem applies, making the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ approximately normal (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates the $z$-score correctly, $z = \frac{190 - 180}{25/\sqrt{40}}$ (1pt); Calculates the correct probability $P(\bar{x} > 190) \approx 0.0057$ (1pt).

FRQ 3

A pharmaceutical company is testing two different formulations of a new allergy medication, Formulation A and Formulation B, to see which is more effective at reducing symptoms. In a randomized controlled trial, 200 patients were given Formulation A and 180 patients were given Formulation B. It is known that Formulation A has a true success rate of $p_A = 0.80$ and Formulation B has a true success rate of $p_B = 0.75$. Assume the samples are independent.
a. Describe the sampling distribution of the difference in sample proportions, $\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B$, including its mean and standard deviation.
b. Justify whether the sampling distribution of $\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B$ can be approximated by a normal distribution.
c. Calculate the probability that the sample proportion for Formulation A is greater than the sample proportion for Formulation B, i.e., $P(\hat{p}_A > \hat{p}_B)$.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Calculates the mean of $\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B$ as $p_A - p_B = 0.80 - 0.75 = 0.05$ (1pt); Calculates the standard deviation of $\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B$ as $\sqrt{\frac{p_A(1-p_A)}{n_A} + \frac{p_B(1-p_B)}{n_B}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.80(0.20)}{200} + \frac{0.75(0.25)}{180}}$ (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Checks all four large counts conditions: $n_A p_A = 200(0.80) = 160 \ge 10$, $n_A(1-p_A) = 200(0.20) = 40 \ge 10$, $n_B p_B = 180(0.75) = 135 \ge 10$, $n_B(1-p_B) = 180(0.25) = 45 \ge 10$, and concludes approximately normal (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates the $z$-score correctly for $P(\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B > 0)$, $z = \frac{0 - (0.80 - 0.75)}{\sqrt{\frac{0.80(0.20)}{200} + \frac{0.75(0.25)}{180}}}$ (1pt); Calculates the correct probability $P(\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B > 0) \approx 0.787$ (1pt).

FRQ 4

A manufacturer of light bulbs wants to estimate the true proportion of defective bulbs produced. They regularly test random samples of bulbs. Instead of using the sample proportion, $\hat{p}$, as an estimator, a new engineer proposes using $p^* = \frac{\text{number of defective bulbs} + 1}{\text{sample size} + 2}$ to account for very small sample sizes or zero defectives.
a. Suppose a random sample of 20 bulbs is taken, and 1 defective bulb is found. Calculate the point estimate for the proportion of defective bulbs using both $\hat{p}$ and $p^*$.
b. Explain what it means for an estimator to be unbiased.
c. Describe how you could use a simulation to estimate the sampling distribution of $p^*$ for samples of size $n=20$, assuming the true proportion of defective bulbs is $p=0.05$.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Calculates $\hat{p} = \frac{1}{20} = 0.05$ and $p^* = \frac{1+1}{20+2} = \frac{2}{22} \approx 0.091$ (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Explains that an estimator is unbiased if the mean (or expected value) of its sampling distribution is equal to the true value of the population parameter being estimated (1pt); provides context, e.g., "on average, the estimator neither overestimates nor underestimates the true parameter" (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Describes simulating many random samples of size 20 (1pt); for each sample, calculates $p^*$ and records the value, then constructs a distribution from these recorded values (1pt).

FRQ 5

A university is comparing the average GPA of students in two different colleges: the College of Arts & Sciences (A) and the College of Engineering (E). From past data, it is known that GPA in College A has a mean of $\mu_A = 3.20$ and a standard deviation of $\sigma_A = 0.35$. GPA in College E has a mean of $\mu_E = 3.05$ and a standard deviation of $\sigma_E = 0.40$. The GPAs are approximately normally distributed in both colleges. A random sample of $n_A = 50$ students is taken from College A and $n_E = 60$ students from College E. Assume the samples are independent.
a. Describe the sampling distribution of the difference in sample means, $\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_E$, including its mean and standard deviation.
b. Justify why the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_E$ can be approximated by a normal distribution.
c. Calculate the probability that the sample mean GPA from College A is less than the sample mean GPA from College E, i.e., $P(\bar{x}_A < \bar{x}_E)$, and interpret this probability in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Calculates the mean of $\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_E$ as $\mu_A - \mu_E = 3.20 - 3.05 = 0.15$ (1pt); Calculates the standard deviation of $\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_E$ as $\sqrt{\frac{\sigma_A^2}{n_A} + \frac{\sigma_E^2}{n_E}} = \sqrt{\frac{(0.35)^2}{50} + \frac{(0.40)^2}{60}}$ (1pt).
  • Part b (1pt): States that since both population distributions are approximately normal, the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_E$ is approximately normal (1pt).
  • Part c (3pts): Calculates the $z$-score correctly for $P(\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_E < 0)$, $z = \frac{0 - (3.20 - 3.05)}{\sqrt{\frac{(0.35)^2}{50} + \frac{(0.40)^2}{60}}}$ (1pt); Calculates the correct probability $P(\bar{x}_A - \bar{x}_E < 0) \approx 0.009$ (1pt); Interprets the probability in context, e.g., "There is a very small probability (about 0.9%) that the sample mean GPA for College A students would be lower than that for College E students, assuming the true population means are 3.20 and 3.05, respectively" (1pt).
