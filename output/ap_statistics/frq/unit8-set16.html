FRQ 1

A candy company claims that its plain M&M's candies are distributed with specific percentages for each color: 24% blue, 20% orange, 16% green, 14% yellow, 13% red, and 13% brown. A student randomly selected a large bag of plain M&M's and counted the number of candies of each color. The observed counts are provided in the table.
ColorBlueOrangeGreenYellowRedBrownTotal
Observed Count384530253230200
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for an appropriate chi-square test to investigate the company's claim.
b. Verify the conditions for performing this chi-square test.
c. Calculate the expected count for yellow M&M's in this sample.
d. The chi-square test statistic was calculated to be $\chi^2 = 8.65$ with a p-value of $0.123$. Interpret the p-value in the context of this problem.
e. Based on the p-value provided in part (d) and a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$, what conclusion should the student make about the company's claim? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States correct null and alternative hypotheses for goodness-of-fit test, including population parameter or distribution of proportions.
  • Part b (1pt): Verifies all three conditions (random sample, large sample/expected counts $\ge 5$, independence) in context.
  • Part c (1pt): Shows correct calculation for the expected count for yellow M&M's ($200 \times 0.14 = 28$).
  • Part d (1pt): Correctly interprets the p-value in context as the probability of observing sample data as or more extreme than what was observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true.
  • Part e (1pt): States correct conclusion (fail to reject $H_0$) with justification based on comparing p-value to $\alpha$ and linking back to the context of the company's claim.

FRQ 2

A pharmaceutical company is testing a new drug designed to alleviate allergy symptoms. They conducted a randomized experiment with 300 volunteers, assigning them to one of three dosage groups: low, medium, or high. After a week, they recorded whether each participant experienced significant symptom relief. The results are summarized in the two-way table.
ReliefNo ReliefTotal
Low Dosage7030100
Medium Dosage8515100
High Dosage6535100
Total22080300
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for an appropriate chi-square test.
b. Identify the specific type of chi-square test that should be performed. Justify your choice.
c. Calculate the expected count of participants in the 'High Dosage' group who experienced 'No Relief'. Show your work.
d. Verify the conditions for performing this chi-square test.
e. The test produced a chi-square statistic of $\chi^2 = 7.15$ and a p-value of $0.028$. Based on these results, with a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$, what conclusion can be drawn about the drug's effectiveness across different dosages? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): States correct null and alternative hypotheses comparing the distributions of symptom relief across dosage groups.
  • Part b (1pt): Identifies the test as a chi-square test for homogeneity and justifies it by explaining that it compares the distribution of a categorical variable (relief) across different populations/groups (dosage levels) from independent samples.
  • Part c (1pt): Shows correct calculation for expected count: $\frac{100 \times 80}{300} \approx 26.67$.
  • Part d (1pt): Verifies all three conditions (random assignment, large sample/expected counts $\ge 5$, independence/10% rule if applicable for sampling).
  • Part e (1pt): States correct conclusion (reject $H_0$) with justification based on comparing p-value to $\alpha$ and linking back to the context of the drug's effectiveness and dosage.

FRQ 4

When conducting a chi-square test for goodness-of-fit or for homogeneity/independence, it is crucial to understand the properties of the chi-square distribution and the conditions under which the test is valid.
a. Describe the shape of a chi-square distribution. Include how the shape changes as the degrees of freedom increase.
b. Explain the 'large sample size' condition for performing a chi-square test. Why is this condition important?
c. A researcher conducts a chi-square test for independence, but discovers that one of the expected counts is $2.3$. Explain how this might affect the validity of the test results.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Describes the chi-square distribution as skewed right and states that it becomes more symmetric (less skewed) as the degrees of freedom increase.
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that the condition requires all expected counts to be at least 5 (or sometimes 10) and justifies its importance by noting that the chi-square distribution approximates the sampling distribution of the test statistic only when expected counts are sufficiently large.
  • Part c (1pt): Explains that an expected count of $2.3$ violates the large sample size condition (expected counts $\ge 5$), which means the chi-square approximation for the sampling distribution may not be accurate, leading to an unreliable p-value and potentially an incorrect conclusion.

FRQ 5

A candy company claims that its bags of mixed candies contain 30% red, 20% orange, 20% yellow, 10% green, and 20% brown candies. A statistics student believes the distribution is different and takes a random sample of 200 candies from a large production batch. The observed counts are given in the table below.
ColorObserved Count
Red70
Orange35
Yellow45
Green20
Brown30
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses for an appropriate significance test to investigate the student's belief.
b. Calculate the expected count for yellow candies in this sample.
c. Verify the conditions for conducting this test.
d. The test statistic for this sample is $\chi^2 = 7.5$. Calculate the p-value for this test. Show your work or describe how you would find it.
e. Based on the p-value from part (d) and a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$, what conclusion should the student make? Justify your answer in context.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly states $H_0$ (specific proportions for each color) and $H_a$ (at least one proportion is different) in context.
  • Part b (1pt): Shows calculation for expected yellow candies: $200 \times 0.20 = 40$.
  • Part c (1pt): Verifies random sample, counts are from a categorical variable, and all expected counts are $\ge 5$.
  • Part d (1pt): Identifies degrees of freedom ($df=4$) and uses $\chi^2cdf(7.5, 10^9, 4)$ to find p-value (approximately $0.111$).
  • Part e (1pt): Compares p-value to $\alpha$, states "fail to reject $H_0$", and concludes there is no convincing evidence that the distribution of candy colors is different from the company's claim.
