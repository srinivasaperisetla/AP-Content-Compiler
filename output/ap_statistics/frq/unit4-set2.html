FRQ 2

A small company tracks the number of technical support calls received per hour during peak times. Based on historical data, the probability distribution for the number of calls, $X$, is given below.
Number of Calls ($X$)Probability $P(X=x)$
00.15
10.25
20.30
3?
40.10
a. Complete the probability distribution table for $X$.
b. Calculate the expected number of technical support calls per hour, $E(X)$. Show your work.
c. Calculate the standard deviation of the number of technical support calls per hour, $\sigma_X$. Show your work.
d. Interpret the expected value from part (b) in the context of this problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly calculates the missing probability for $X=3$ as $1 - (0.15+0.25+0.30+0.10) = 0.20$.
  • Part b (1pt): Correctly calculates $E(X) = \sum x \cdot P(X=x) = (0 \cdot 0.15) + (1 \cdot 0.25) + (2 \cdot 0.30) + (3 \cdot 0.20) + (4 \cdot 0.10) = 1.85$.
  • Part c (2pts): Correctly calculates $\sigma_X = \sqrt{\sum (x-E(X))^2 P(X=x)} \approx 1.117$.
  • Part d (1pt): Provides a correct interpretation of $E(X)$ in context, stating it is the long-run average number of calls per hour.

FRQ 3

A quality control manager at a manufacturing plant knows that 15% of the items produced by a certain machine are defective. A random sample of 8 items produced by this machine is selected for inspection. Assume the items are independent.
a. Justify that this situation can be modeled by a binomial distribution.
b. Calculate the probability that exactly 2 of the 8 selected items are defective. Show your work.
c. Calculate the mean and standard deviation of the number of defective items in a sample of 8. Show your work.
d. What is the probability that at least one of the 8 selected items is defective?

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the four conditions for a binomial distribution (Binary outcomes, Independent trials, Fixed number of trials, Same probability of success) and states how they are met in this context.
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $P(X=2) = \binom{8}{2} (0.15)^2 (0.85)^6 \approx 0.209$. Shows the formula or setup.
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates mean $\mu_X = n p = 8(0.15) = 1.2$ and standard deviation $\sigma_X = \sqrt{np(1-p)} = \sqrt{8(0.15)(0.85)} \approx 1.01$. Shows formulas or setup.
  • Part d (1pt): Calculates $P(X \ge 1) = 1 - P(X=0) = 1 - \binom{8}{0} (0.15)^0 (0.85)^8 = 1 - (0.85)^8 \approx 0.7275$.

FRQ 4

A game involves rolling a fair six-sided die repeatedly until a 3 appears. Let $Y$ be the number of rolls required to get the first 3.
a. Explain why $Y$ follows a geometric distribution.
b. Calculate the probability that it takes exactly 4 rolls to get the first 3. Show your work.
c. Calculate the probability that it takes more than 5 rolls to get the first 3. Show your work.
d. Calculate the expected number of rolls needed to get the first 3. Interpret this value in the context of the game.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Explains the conditions for a geometric distribution (trials are independent, probability of success is constant, counting trials until the first success).
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates $P(Y=4) = (5/6)^3 (1/6) \approx 0.09645$. Shows the formula or setup.
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates $P(Y > 5) = (5/6)^5 \approx 0.40188$. Shows the formula or setup.
  • Part d (2pts): Calculates $E(Y) = 1/p = 1/(1/6) = 6$. Provides a correct interpretation: on average, it will take 6 rolls to get the first 3 in the long run.

FRQ 5

Number of Cakes Sold (x)Probability P(X=x)
00.10
10.25
20.35
30.20
40.10
a. Construct a probability histogram for the distribution of $X$. Label the axes clearly.
b. Calculate the expected number of cakes sold per day, $E(X)$.
c. Interpret the value of $E(X)$ in the context of the problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly constructs a histogram with appropriate scales and labels on both axes (e.g., $x$ on horizontal axis, $P(X=x)$ on vertical axis) and correct bar heights for each $x$ value.
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates $E(X) = \sum x \cdot P(X=x) = (0)(0.10) + (1)(0.25) + (2)(0.35) + (3)(0.20) + (4)(0.10) = 1.95$ cakes.
  • Part c (1pt): Interprets $E(X)$ as the long-run average number of cakes sold per day. If the bakery observes sales over many days, the average number of cakes sold per day will be approximately 1.95.
