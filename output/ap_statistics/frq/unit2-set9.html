AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 2: Exploring Two-Variable Data (Set 9)

FRQ 1

A real estate agent recorded the selling price (in thousands of dollars) and the size (in square feet) for 10 homes in a particular neighborhood. A scatterplot of the data is shown below. The correlation coefficient for these data is $r = 0.88$.
a. Describe the direction, form, and strength of the relationship between the selling price and the size of homes in this neighborhood based on the scatterplot.
b. Interpret the value of the correlation coefficient $r = 0.88$ in the context of this problem.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (3pts): Identifies the direction as positive, the form as linear, and the strength as strong (1 pt each).
  • Part b (2pts): States that there is a strong, positive linear association between home size and selling price (1 pt), and explains that larger homes tend to command higher selling prices (1 pt for context).

FRQ 2

A researcher is studying the relationship between the number of hours a student spends studying for a final exam ($x$) and their score on the exam ($y$). Data from a random sample of students were collected, and a least-squares regression line was calculated. The equation of the line is $\hat{y} = 58.5 + 2.5x$. The coefficient of determination is $r^2 = 0.64$.
a. Interpret the slope of the least-squares regression line in the context of this problem.
b. Interpret the $y$-intercept of the least-squares regression line in the context of this problem. Does the $y$-intercept have a practical meaning in this context?
c. Predict the score for a student who studies 10 hours for the exam.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Interprets the slope as an increase of 2.5 points in exam score for each additional hour of studying.
  • Part b (2pts): Interprets the $y$-intercept as an expected score of 58.5 for a student who studies 0 hours (1 pt), and discusses whether this is practical or makes sense in context (e.g., a student studying 0 hours might still get 58.5 points, which is a plausible baseline score) (1 pt).
  • Part c (1pt): Calculates $\hat{y} = 58.5 + 2.5(10) = 83.5$ (1 pt).

FRQ 3

A manufacturer of light bulbs is investigating the relationship between the age of a bulb (in hours) and its brightness (in lumens). A least-squares regression line was fitted to the data, and the residual plot is shown below. One bulb, used for 1200 hours, had an actual brightness of 850 lumens, while the regression line predicted its brightness to be 870 lumens.
a. Describe what the residual plot indicates about the appropriateness of a linear model for predicting brightness from age.
b. Calculate the residual for the bulb that was used for 1200 hours.
c. Based on the residual plot, would you recommend using the linear model to predict brightness? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): States that there is no obvious pattern (e.g., no curve, no fanning) in the residual plot (1 pt), and that the residuals are scattered randomly around zero (1 pt).
  • Part b (1pt): Calculates the residual as $Actual - Predicted = 850 - 870 = -20$ lumens.
  • Part c (2pts): Recommends using the linear model (1 pt) and justifies by referencing the lack of a pattern in the residual plot and the random scatter around zero, indicating a linear model is appropriate (1 pt).

FRQ 5

A study investigated the relationship between the weight (in pounds) of a car ($x$) and its fuel efficiency (miles per gallon, $y$). The initial scatterplot showed a strong negative linear relationship, but one very heavy car with unusually high fuel efficiency seemed to be an outlier. After removing this point, a new least-squares regression line was calculated for the remaining 24 cars, yielding $\log(\hat{y}) = 2.1 - 0.00005x$.
a. Explain how removing the outlier (heavy car with high fuel efficiency) would likely affect the correlation coefficient ($r$) and the slope of the regression line. Would it strengthen or weaken the correlation? Would the slope become more or less negative?
b. Using the new regression line, predict the fuel efficiency (in miles per gallon) for a car that weighs 3500 pounds. Show your work.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): States that removing the outlier would likely strengthen the correlation (make $r$ closer to -1) (1 pt) and make the slope more negative (steeper negative slope) (1 pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates $\log(\hat{y}) = 2.1 - 0.00005(3500) = 2.1 - 0.175 = 1.925$ (1 pt). Then calculates $\hat{y} = 10^{1.925} \approx 84.14$ miles per gallon (1 pt).
