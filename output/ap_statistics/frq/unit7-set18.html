FRQ 3

A manufacturer of mobile phones claims that the average battery life of its newest model is 24 hours under normal usage. A consumer advocacy group suspects that the average battery life is less than 24 hours. They randomly select 15 new phones and test their battery life under controlled conditions, recording the following sample data (in hours): 22.5, 23.1, 21.9, 23.8, 22.0, 22.9, 23.5, 21.5, 22.8, 23.0, 22.4, 21.8, 23.3, 22.7, 22.2. The sample mean battery life is $\bar{x} = 22.76$ hours and the sample standard deviation is $s = 0.73$ hours.
a. Identify the appropriate inference procedure to estimate the true mean battery life of these phones. Verify the conditions for this procedure.
b. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true mean battery life of the phones.
c. Interpret the confidence interval in the context of the problem.
d. Based on this confidence interval, does the data support the manufacturer's claim that the average battery life is 24 hours? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies a one-sample t-interval for a population mean (1pt). Verifies conditions: Random sample (given), $n=15 < 30$ so check normality (assume approximately normal from past data/sample not extremely skewed; or note small sample size implies a concern if not normal) (1pt).
  • Part b (2pts): Calculates degrees of freedom $df=14$ and critical $t^*=2.145$ (1pt). Constructs interval $22.76 \pm 2.145 \left(\frac{0.73}{\sqrt{15}}\right) = 22.76 \pm 0.404 = (22.356, 23.164)$ (1pt).
  • Part c (1pt): We are 95% confident that the true mean battery life of this model of phone is between 22.356 hours and 23.164 hours.
  • Part d (1pt): No, the interval does not contain 24 hours. Since 24 hours is not in the interval, we have significant evidence that the true mean battery life is not 24 hours (and likely less).

FRQ 4

A food company claims that the mean weight of its 16-ounce cereal boxes is at least 16 ounces. A consumer watchdog organization is concerned that the company is underfilling the boxes. They randomly select 20 boxes of cereal and find the mean weight to be 15.8 ounces with a standard deviation of 0.5 ounces.
a. State the null and alternative hypotheses to test the watchdog organization's concern.
b. Identify the appropriate inference procedure and verify the conditions for this test.
c. Calculate the test statistic and the p-value for this test.
d. Based on the p-value, what conclusion can be drawn at the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): $H_0: \mu = 16$ ounces versus $H_a: \mu < 16$ ounces, where $\mu$ is the true mean weight of the cereal boxes.
  • Part b (2pts): Identifies a one-sample t-test for a population mean (1pt). Verifies conditions: Random sample (given). $n=20 < 30$, so check normality (assume approximately normal based on context/sample not extremely skewed or note concern about normality) (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Test statistic $t = \frac{15.8 - 16}{0.5/\sqrt{20}} = \frac{-0.2}{0.1118} \approx -1.789$ (1pt). P-value $P(t < -1.789)$ with $df=19 \approx 0.044$ (1pt).
  • Part d (1pt): Since the p-value (0.044) is less than $\alpha = 0.05$, we reject the null hypothesis. There is sufficient evidence to conclude that the true mean weight of the cereal boxes is less than 16 ounces.

FRQ 5

A tire manufacturer wants to compare the tread wear of two different rubber compounds, Compound A and Compound B. Instead of using two separate groups of cars, they decide to use 10 cars and install one tire made with Compound A and one tire made with Compound B on the rear wheels of each car (randomly assigning sides). After 20,000 miles, the tread wear (in mm) for each tire is measured, and the difference (Compound A - Compound B) is calculated for each car. The differences are: 1.2, 0.8, -0.1, 0.5, 0.3, 1.0, 0.6, 0.7, 0.2, 0.4. The mean difference is $\bar{x}_d = 0.56$ mm and the standard deviation of the differences is $s_d = 0.38$ mm.
a. Explain why a paired t-procedure is appropriate for this study design.
b. Verify the conditions for constructing a confidence interval for the mean difference in tread wear.
c. Construct a 90% confidence interval for the true mean difference in tread wear between Compound A and Compound B.
d. Based on the interval, is there convincing evidence of a difference in tread wear between the two compounds? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): A paired t-procedure is appropriate because the measurements are taken on the same cars, creating dependent (paired) observations. This controls for car-to-car variability.
  • Part b (1pt): Conditions for paired t-interval: Random assignment of tires to sides of cars for each pair, which ensures independence of differences (implicit in random assignment within pairs) (given). $n=10 < 30$, so check normality of differences (assume approximately normal as sample not extremely skewed, or note need for larger sample/normality check) (1pt).
  • Part c (2pts): Calculates $df=9$ and critical $t^*=1.833$ (1pt). Constructs interval $0.56 \pm 1.833 \left(\frac{0.38}{\sqrt{10}}\right) = 0.56 \pm 0.220 = (0.340, 0.780)$ mm (1pt).
  • Part d (1pt): Yes, there is convincing evidence. Since the entire 90% confidence interval $(0.340, 0.780)$ is above 0, it suggests that Compound A has a significantly higher mean tread wear (less wear) than Compound B.
