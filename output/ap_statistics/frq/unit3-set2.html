AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 2)

FRQ 1

A research team investigated the relationship between the amount of time high school students spend on extracurricular activities and their academic performance (measured by GPA). They surveyed 500 randomly selected high school students, collecting data on their weekly extracurricular hours and their current GPA.
a. Identify the type of study described. Justify your answer.
b. Based on this study design, can the research team conclude that spending more time on extracurricular activities causes an improvement in academic performance? Explain why or why not.
c. Describe a potential confounding variable in this study and explain how it could affect the observed relationship between extracurricular activities and academic performance.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Correctly identifies the study type as an observational study and provides a correct justification (no random assignment, no manipulation of variables).
  • Part b (2pts): States that a causal conclusion cannot be made and explains that observational studies do not allow for cause-and-effect conclusions due to potential confounding variables.
  • Part c (2pts): Identifies a plausible confounding variable (e.g., family income, motivation, prior academic ability) and explains its relationship to both extracurriculars and GPA, showing how it could create a spurious association.

FRQ 2

A local newspaper wants to estimate the proportion of residents in a city who support a new tax for public library funding. They decide to survey 200 residents.
a. The newspaper sends reporters to a popular shopping mall on a Saturday afternoon and asks the first 200 willing residents they encounter to complete the survey. Identify the sampling method used.
b. Identify a potential source of bias in the sampling method described in part (a) and explain how this bias might affect the estimated proportion of residents who support the new tax.
c. Describe a different sampling method that would likely produce a more representative sample of the city's residents.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Identifies the sampling method as convenience sampling.
  • Part b (2pts): Identifies a relevant bias (e.g., selection bias, voluntary response bias, undercoverage of non-shoppers) and explains how it would lead to an over- or underestimation of support for the tax.
  • Part c (2pts): Describes a valid probability sampling method (e.g., simple random sample, stratified random sample, systematic random sample) in sufficient detail.

FRQ 3

A company produces a new fertilizer and wants to test its effectiveness on tomato plant growth. They have 60 similar tomato plants available for an experiment. They want to compare the new fertilizer (Fertilizer A) against their old fertilizer (Fertilizer B) and a control group (no fertilizer).
a. Identify the experimental units, the treatments, and the response variable for this experiment.
b. Describe how the company could use a completely randomized design to conduct this experiment.
c. Explain one advantage of using blocking in an experiment like this, and suggest a blocking variable that would be appropriate.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly identifies experimental units (tomato plants), treatments (Fertilizer A, Fertilizer B, No Fertilizer), and response variable (e.g., plant growth, height, yield).
  • Part b (2pts): Describes a completely randomized design including random assignment of the 60 plants to the three treatment groups with appropriate numbers per group.
  • Part c (2pts): Explains an advantage of blocking (e.g., reduces variability, accounts for known sources of variation) and suggests a relevant blocking variable (e.g., location in greenhouse, plant size, sunlight exposure).

FRQ 4

A pharmaceutical company is developing a new nasal spray to alleviate allergy symptoms. They want to compare the new spray (Drug X) to a placebo. They recruit 300 volunteers who suffer from seasonal allergies.
a. Describe how the company should incorporate both a placebo and blinding (single-blind and double-blind) into the design of this experiment. Explain the purpose of each.
b. After conducting the experiment, the company analyzes the data and finds that participants who used Drug X reported significantly fewer allergy symptoms than those who used the placebo. Interpret what "significantly fewer" means in this context.
c. Can the company conclude that Drug X *caused* a reduction in allergy symptoms? Justify your answer.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (3pts): Describes the use of a placebo and explains its purpose (control for psychological effect); describes single-blinding (participants unaware) and double-blinding (both participants and researchers/evaluators unaware) and explains their purposes (reduce bias).
  • Part b (1pt): Interprets "significantly fewer" to mean that the observed difference is unlikely to have occurred by random chance alone if Drug X had no effect.
  • Part c (2pts): States that a causal conclusion can be drawn due to the well-designed randomized experiment (control, randomization, blinding) and explains that this design helps eliminate confounding variables.

FRQ 5

A small town council is considering building a new community center. They want to gather public opinion before making a decision.
a. Identify two distinct statistical questions the town council could investigate to gather public opinion about the new community center.
b. The town council decides to place a survey link on their official website and encourage residents to participate. Identify one potential source of bias associated with this data collection method and explain its impact on the results.
c. Suggest a modification to the data collection method in part (b) that would help reduce the bias you identified, and explain why your modification would be effective.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies two clear statistical questions related to public opinion on the community center (e.g., "What proportion of residents support the new center?", "What is the average amount residents are willing to pay for membership?").
  • Part b (2pts): Identifies voluntary response bias or selection bias/undercoverage (only residents with internet access who visit the site) and explains how it would lead to an over- or underestimation of support, or a non-representative sample.
  • Part c (2pts): Suggests a reasonable modification (e.g., random phone survey, stratified random survey of households) and explains how it would address the identified bias by providing a more representative sample.
