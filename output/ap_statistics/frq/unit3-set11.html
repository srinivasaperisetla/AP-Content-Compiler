AP Statistics FRQs — Unit 3: Collecting Data (Set 11)

FRQ 1

A large university wants to conduct a survey to understand student satisfaction with on-campus housing facilities. There are 20,000 students living in 10 different dormitories. The university staff wants to select a sample of 200 students.
a. Describe how to select a simple random sample (SRS) of 200 students from all 20,000 students living in on-campus housing.
b. Explain one advantage of using a stratified random sample over an SRS in this context, where each dormitory is a stratum.
c. Identify one potential source of bias if the university were to survey only students who volunteer to participate in the survey via an email link.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Provides a clear, step-by-step description of how to select an SRS (e.g., assign numbers, use a random number generator).
  • Part b (2pts): Explains that a stratified random sample ensures representation from each dorm, which might reveal differences in satisfaction levels across dorms or reduce variability within estimates if dorms are different. It also explains how this is an advantage over SRS in this context.
  • Part c (1pt): Identifies voluntary response bias or nonresponse bias, and briefly explains how it might lead to over- or under-estimation of satisfaction.

FRQ 2

Researchers are interested in whether regular consumption of green tea reduces the risk of heart disease. They recruit 1,000 participants and collect data on their green tea consumption habits and their medical history, specifically focusing on the incidence of heart disease over a 10-year period.
a. Identify the type of study (observational study or experiment) described in this scenario and justify your choice.
b. Based on this study, can the researchers conclude that regular green tea consumption causes a reduction in the risk of heart disease? Explain why or why not.
c. Describe how the researchers could design an experiment to investigate if green tea consumption causes a reduction in heart disease risk.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Identifies it as an observational study because researchers are observing characteristics and not imposing treatments. Justification must mention no treatment imposed by researchers.
  • Part b (1pt): States no, and explains that observational studies cannot establish cause-and-effect relationships due to potential confounding variables.
  • Part c (2pts): Describes a basic experimental design including random assignment of participants to at least two groups (e.g., green tea consumption group and a control group), and specifies a comparison of heart disease incidence.

FRQ 3

A pet food company wants to test a new dog food formula designed to improve coat health. They recruit 60 dogs of similar breed, age, and health condition for a study. Half of the dogs will receive the new formula, and the other half will receive their regular dog food for three months. A veterinarian, unaware of which food each dog is receiving, will assess the coat health of each dog at the end of the study.
a. Identify the experimental units, the treatments, and the response variable in this experiment.
b. Explain the purpose of using a control group (dogs receiving regular food) in this experiment.
c. Explain how random assignment would be used in this experiment to form the two groups of dogs.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Correctly identifies experimental units as the 60 dogs, treatments as the new dog food formula and the regular dog food (control), and the response variable as coat health.
  • Part b (1pt): Explains that the control group provides a baseline for comparison to determine if the new formula has an effect beyond what might naturally occur or from other factors.
  • Part c (2pts): Describes a method for randomly assigning the 60 dogs to the two food groups, such as numbering the dogs from 1 to 60 and using a random number generator to select 30 for the new formula, with the remaining 30 receiving regular food.

FRQ 4

An agricultural scientist wants to compare the effectiveness of two new organic pesticides (Pesticide A and Pesticide B) against a control (no pesticide) in preventing aphid infestations on tomato plants. They have 30 identical tomato plants.
a. Describe how to design a completely randomized experiment for this study, including how the pesticides would be assigned to the plants.
b. Describe how to design a randomized block experiment for this study, assuming the plants are grown in three different greenhouse sections with slightly varying light conditions.
c. Compare the two designs, explaining which design would be more appropriate for this study and justifying your choice.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (2pts): Describes a completely randomized design: random assignment of 30 plants to 3 treatment groups (10 plants per group: Pesticide A, Pesticide B, Control). For example, number plants 1-30, use a random number generator to select 10 for A, then 10 for B, remaining 10 for Control.
  • Part b (2pts): Describes a randomized block design: dividing the 30 plants into 3 blocks of 10 plants each (based on greenhouse section). Within each block, randomly assign the 3 treatments (Pesticide A, Pesticide B, Control) to an equal number of plants (e.g., 3-4 plants per treatment per block).
  • Part c (2pts): Compares the two designs, noting that the randomized block design (part b) is more appropriate because it accounts for the variability due to differing light conditions (blocking variable), reducing variability within treatment groups and making it easier to detect treatment effects. Justification should link blocking to controlling for a known source of variation.

FRQ 5

A study was conducted to determine if a new teaching method significantly improves student test scores in mathematics compared to the traditional method. A well-designed randomized experiment was performed with two groups of students. After the study, the average test score for students using the new method was 85, and for the traditional method, it was 80. A statistical analysis reported a p-value of $0.015$.
a. Interpret the p-value of $0.015$ in the context of this study.
b. Based on the p-value, what conclusion can be drawn about the effectiveness of the new teaching method at a significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$? Justify your conclusion.
c. Can the researchers conclude that the new teaching method *caused* the observed improvement in test scores? Explain why or why not.

Scoring Guidelines

  • Part a (1pt): Interprets the p-value as the probability of observing a difference in average test scores as extreme as or more extreme than 5 points (85-80), assuming there is no actual difference between the new and traditional teaching methods.
  • Part b (2pts): Concludes that there is sufficient evidence to support that the new teaching method significantly improves test scores. Justifies this by stating that since $0.015 < 0.05$, the null hypothesis of no difference should be rejected.
  • Part c (1pt): States that yes, causality can be concluded. Explains that because this was a well-designed randomized experiment, it controls for confounding variables, allowing for a cause-and-effect conclusion.
